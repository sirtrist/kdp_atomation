As on his previous visit, Raskolnikov saw the door open very slightly and, through the narrow opening, two bright eyes fixed on him with a look of distrust. Then his coolness left him, and he committed a mistake which might have ruined everything.

Fearing that Alena Ivanovna might be afraid of being alone with a visitor whose appearance must have been far from reassuring, he seized the door and pulled it to him, so that the old woman would not think of closing it again. The usurer did not try it, but she did not let go of the doorknob either, so that she almost fell out of the anteroom onto the square when Raskolnikov pulled the door to him. As she stood on the threshold and refused to let him pass, he walked straight up to her. Frightened, she jumped back, tried to speak, but could not utter a word, and looked at the young man with her eyes wide open.

"Good morning, Alena Ivanovna," he began, in as unconcerned a tone as he could assume, but in vain did he affect indifference; his voice was broken and trembling; "I bring you... an object... but come in... to judge of it, you must see it in the light... " And without waiting to be invited in, he entered the room. The old woman followed him quickly, her tongue loosened.

— Lord! But what do you want? ... Who are you? What do you need?

"Come, Alena Ivanovna, you know me well... Raskolnikov... Here, I bring you the pledge I spoke to you about the other day..." And he held out the object to her.

Alena Ivanovna was about to examine him, when suddenly she changed her mind and, raising her eyes, fixed a piercing, irritated, and suspicious look on the visitor who had entered her house with so little ceremony. She looked at him for a minute. Raskolnikov even thought he saw a kind of mockery in the old woman's eyes, as if she had already guessed everything. He felt that he was losing his composure, that he was almost afraid, and that if this silent inquisition lasted another half minute, he would probably take flight.

"What are you staring at me like that for, as if you didn't recognize me?" he said suddenly, getting angry in his turn. "If you want this object, take it; if you don't, that's fine, I'll go elsewhere; it's useless to make me waste my time."

These words escaped him without his having in any way premeditated them.

The visitor's resolute language made an excellent impression on the old woman.

"But why are you so hurried, batuchka? What is it?" she asked, looking at the pledge.

— A silver cigarette case: I told you last time.

She held out her hand.

"How pale you are! Your hands are trembling! You are ill, batuchka?"

"I have fever," he replied in a broken voice. "How could one not be pale... when one has nothing to eat?" he finished with some difficulty. His strength was leaving him again. But the answer seemed plausible; the old woman took the pledge.

"What is it?" she asked for the second time, and, while weighing the pledge, she again fixed her eyes on her interlocutor.

— An object… a cigarette case… made of silver… look.

While Alena Ivanovna was trying to untie the little parcel, she had approached the light (all her windows were closed, despite the stifling heat); in this position, she turned her back to Raskolnikov, and for a few seconds she did not pay any attention to him. The young man unbuttoned his coat and freed the axe from the noose, but without removing it completely; he simply held it in his right hand under his clothes. A terrible weakness invaded his limbs; he himself felt that they were becoming more and more numb. He was afraid that his fingers would let go of the axe... suddenly his head began to spin.

"But what has he put in there?" cried Alena Ivanovna angrily, and she made a movement towards Raskolnikov.

There was no time to lose. He took the axe out from under his coat, raised it in the air with both hands, and, with a limp, almost mechanical gesture, for he had no strength left, let it fall on the old woman's head. However, as soon as he had struck, his physical energy returned.

Alena Ivanovna, as usual, was bareheaded. Her grayish, scanty hair, always greasy with oil, was gathered in a thin plait, called a rat's tail, and fastened at the nape of the neck with a piece of horn comb. She uttered a faint cry and suddenly sank to the floor; but she still had the strength to raise both arms to her head. In one of her hands she still held the "pawn." At the moment of the fall the young man had stepped back; as soon as he saw the old woman lying on the floor, he bent over her face; she was dead. Her eyes, wide open, seemed to be starting out of their sockets, the convulsions of agony had given her whole face a grimacing expression.

The murderer laid the axe on the floor, and, without losing a moment, began to search the corpse, taking the most minute precautions not to soil himself with blood; he remembered that Alena Ivanovna had, on the last occasion, looked for her keys in the right-hand pocket of her dress. He had full possession of his faculties; he felt neither giddiness nor faintness, but his hands continued to tremble. Later on he remembered that he had been very careful, very attentive, and that he had taken every precaution not to soil himself. The keys were not long in being found; they were all together, as the day before, on a steel ring.

Having possessed himself of it, Raskolnikov immediately passed into the bedroom. This room was very small; on one side was a large cupboard filled with holy images, on the other a large, very clean bed with a silk quilt lined with wadding and made of patchwork. Against the third wall stood a chest of drawers. Strange to say, as soon as the young man began to open this piece of furniture, as soon as he began to use the keys, a sort of shiver ran through his whole body. The idea suddenly occurred to him to give up his task and go away, but this impulse lasted only an instant: it was too late to go away.

He even smiled at himself for having thought of such a thing when, suddenly, he was seized with a terrible anxiety: what if the old woman was not yet dead, what if she was regaining consciousness? Leaving the keys and the chest of drawers there, he ran quickly to the body, seized the axe and prepared to strike his victim again, but the weapon, already raised, did not fall: there was no doubt that Alena Ivanovna was dead. Bending over her again to examine her more closely, Raskolnikov saw that her skull was smashed. A pool of blood had formed on the floor. Suddenly noticing a cord around the old woman's neck, the young man pulled it violently, but the bloody cord was strong and did not break.

The murderer then tried to remove it by sliding it along the body. He was no more successful in this second attempt, the cord encountered an obstacle and refused to slide. Impatient, Raskolnikoff brandished the axe, ready to strike the corpse to cut the damned lace at the same time; however, he could not bring himself to proceed with this brutality. Finally, after two minutes of effort, which reddened his hands, he managed to cut the cord with the edge of the axe, without damaging the body of the dead woman. As he had supposed, it was a purse that the old woman wore around her neck. There was also, hanging from the cord, a small enameled medal and two crosses, one made of cypress wood, the other made of copper. The dirty purse, a small chamois leather bag, was absolutely full. Raskolnikoff put it in his pocket without checking the contents; he threw the crosses on the old woman's chest, and, taking the axe with him this time, he hurried back into the bedroom.

His impatience was extreme; he seized the keys and set to work again. But his attempts to open the chest were unsuccessful, which was due less to the trembling of his hands than to his continual mistakes; he saw, for example, that such a key did not fit the lock, and yet he persisted in trying to insert it. Suddenly he recalled a conjecture he had already made during his previous visit: this large key with a serrated bit, which was hanging with the others around the steel ring, must be the key not of the chest, but of some box in which the old woman had perhaps put all her valuables. Without further bothering about the chest, he immediately looked under the bed, knowing that old women are in the habit of hiding their treasure in that place.

There was indeed a chest, about three feet long, covered with red morocco. The jagged key fitted perfectly into the lock. When Raskolnikov opened the chest, he saw a hareskin coat with a red lining lying on a white sheet; under the fur was a silk dress, then a shawl; the bottom seemed to contain only rags. The young man began by wiping his bloody hands on the red lining. "The blood will show less on red." Then he suddenly came to his senses: "Lord, am I going mad?" he thought with horror.

But scarcely had he touched these rags when a gold watch slipped out from under the fur. He began to turn over the contents of the chest. Among the rags were gold objects, which had probably all been deposited as pledges in the hands of the usurer, – bracelets, chains, earrings, tiepins, etc. Some were enclosed in boxes, others tied with a ribbon in a folded-up piece of newspaper.

Raskolnikov did not hesitate, he seized the jewels, and filled his trousers and overcoat pockets with them without opening the boxes or undoing the parcels; but he was soon interrupted in his work.

Footsteps were audible in the room where the old woman lay. He stopped, frozen with terror. However, the noise having ceased, he already believed he had been the victim of an auditory hallucination, when suddenly he distinctly perceived a slight cry, or rather a sort of weak and intermittent moan. After one or two minutes, everything fell back into a deathly silence. Raskolnikov had sat down on the floor near the chest and waited, barely breathing. Suddenly he leapt up, seized the axe, and rushed out of the bedroom.

In the middle of the room, Elizabeth, with a large bundle in her hands, was gazing with a terrified eye at the corpse of her sister; pale as a sheet, she seemed to have no strength to cry out. At the sudden appearance of the murderer, she began to tremble in every limb, and shivers ran over her face: she tried to raise her arm, to open her mouth, but she uttered no cry, and, slowly retreating, her eyes still fixed on Raskolnikoff, she went and huddled in a corner. The poor woman made this retreat without crying out, as if the breath had failed in her breast. The young man rushed upon her, the axe uplifted: the lips of the unfortunate woman assumed the plaintive expression that one notices in very young children when they begin to be afraid of something, stare fixedly at the object that frightens them, and are on the point of crying out.

Terror had so completely stupefied the unfortunate Elizabeth that, threatened by the axe, she did not even think of protecting her face by putting her hands before her head in that mechanical gesture suggested in such cases by the instinct of self-preservation. She barely raised her left arm and slowly extended it in the direction of the murderer as if to push him away. The iron of the axe penetrated the skull, split the entire upper part of the forehead and almost reached the sinciput. Elizabeth fell dead. Not knowing what he was doing, Raskolnikov took the package that the victim was holding in her hand, then abandoned it and ran to the anteroom.

He was more and more terrified, especially since this second murder, which he had not in the least premeditated. He was eager to escape; if he had then been able to understand things better, if he had only been able to calculate all the difficulties of his position, to see it as desperate, as ugly, as absurd as it was, to understand how many obstacles he still had to overcome, perhaps even crimes to commit to tear himself away from this house and return home, he would very likely have given up the struggle and gone straight to denounce himself; it was not even cowardice that would have driven him to do so, but the horror of what he had done. This impression was growing stronger by the minute. For nothing in the world would he have wanted to approach the box now, or even go back into the apartment.

However, little by little his mind was distracted by other thoughts, and he fell into a sort of reverie; at times the murderer seemed to forget himself, or rather to forget the main thing in order to attach himself to trifles. Besides, a glance thrown into the kitchen having revealed to him on a bench a bucket half full of water, he had the idea of washing his hands and his axe. The blood made his hands sticky. After dipping the edge of the axe in the water, he took a small piece of soap that was on the window sill and began to make his ablutions. When he had washed his hands, he set about cleaning the iron of his weapon; then he spent three minutes soaping the wood, which had also received splashes of blood.

Then he wiped everything with a cloth that was drying on a rope stretched across the kitchen. This task completed, he approached the window to examine the axe attentively and at length. The incriminating traces had disappeared, but the wood was still damp. Raskolnikov carefully hid the weapon under his overcoat by putting it back in the noose; after which, he made a meticulous inspection of his clothes, as much as the dim light that lit the kitchen allowed him. At first glance, the trousers and overcoat did not offer anything suspicious, but there were stains on the boots. He removed them with a cloth soaked in water.

These precautions, however, only half reassured him, for he knew that his sight was poor and that he might very well have failed to notice certain stains. He stood irresolute in the middle of the room, a prey to a dark, anguishing thought: the thought that he was going mad, that at that moment he was incapable of making up his mind and of taking care of his safety, that his way of acting was perhaps not the right one in the present circumstances... "My God! I must go away, go away as quickly as possible!" he murmured, and he rushed into the antechamber, where the worst terror he had yet experienced awaited him.

He remained motionless, not daring to believe his eyes: the door of the apartment, the outer door that gave access to the square, the very one where he had rung earlier and through which he had entered, he found it open: until this moment, it had remained ajar; perhaps, the old woman had not closed it again; no one had turned a key or pulled the bolt! But, my God! he had seen Elisabeth afterwards! How then had he not suspected that she had entered through the door? She could not have entered the apartment by crossing the wall.

He closed the door and locked it.

"No, that's not it yet!" he said. "I must go, I must go!" He pulled the bolt and, after opening the door, listened on the stairs.

He listened attentively for a long time. Downstairs, probably under the gateway, two loud voices were exchanging insults. "What kind of people are these?" He waited patiently. Finally the shouting stopped: the two brawlers had gone their separate ways. The young man was about to leave when, on the floor below, a door opened with a bang onto the stairs and someone started to descend, humming a tune.

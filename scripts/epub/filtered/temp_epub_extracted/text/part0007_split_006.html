That same day, between six and seven o'clock in the evening, Raskolnikov went to his mother and sister. The two women now lived in the Bakaleyeff house, the apartment Razumikhin had told them about. As he climbed the stairs, Raskolnikov seemed to hesitate again. However, for nothing in the world would he have turned back; he was determined to make this visit. "Besides, they don't know anything yet," he thought, "and they are already used to seeing me as an original." His clothes were covered in mud and torn; on the other hand, physical fatigue, combined with the struggle that had been going on inside him for nearly twenty-four hours, had made his face almost unrecognizable. The young man had spent the whole night God knows where. But, at least, his mind was made up.

He knocked at the door, and his mother opened it. Dunyasha was out, and the servant was not at home at the moment. Pulcheria Alexandrovna was at first silent with surprise and joy; then she took her son by the hand and led him into the room.

Ah! you are here! she said in a voice that trembled with emotion.

“Don't be angry, Rodya, if I am foolish enough to greet you with tears; it is happiness that makes them flow. You think I am sad? No, I am gay, I am laughing, only I have this foolish habit of shedding tears. Since your father's death, I cry like that about everything. Sit down, my dear, you are tired, I can see. Ah! how dirty you are!”

"I got wet yesterday, mother," began Raskolnikov.

“Leave it alone!” Pulcheria Alexandrovna interrupted him quickly. “You thought I was going to question you with my old woman's curiosity? Be at ease, I understand, I understand everything; now I am a little initiated into the ways of Petersburg, and I really see for myself that people are more intelligent here than in our town. I have said to myself once and for all that I have no need to meddle in your affairs and ask you for an account. While you are perhaps occupied with God knows what thoughts, I would go and disturb you with my importunate questions!… Ah, Lord!… You see, Rodya? I am reading for the third time the article you published in a journal, Dmitri Prokofitch brought it to me. It was a revelation to me; from that moment, indeed, I understood everything and recognized how stupid I had been. “That's what he is occupied with,” I said to myself; “he is hatching new ideas in his head, and he doesn't like to be interrupted in his reflections; all scholars are like that.” In spite of the attention with which I read you, my friend, there are many things in your article that escape me; but, ignorant as I am, I have no reason to be surprised if I don't understand everything.

— Show it to me, Mother.

Raskolnikov took the number of the journal and glanced rapidly over his article. An author always experiences a keen pleasure in seeing himself in print for the first time, especially when the author is only twenty-three years old. Though he was in the midst of the most cruel anxiety, our hero could not escape this feeling, but it did not last long. After reading a few lines, he frowned, and a terrible anguish seized his heart. This reading had suddenly recalled to him all the moral agitations of the last few months. It was with a feeling of violent repulsion that he threw the brochure on the table.

"But, though I am a simpleton, Rodya, I can see that in a very short time you will occupy one of the first places, if not the first, in the world of science. And they dared to think you were mad! Ha! ha! ha! You don't know that they had such an idea? Ah! the poor things! And how could they understand what intelligence is? To think that Dounia herself, yes, Dounia herself, was not far from believing it! Is it possible! Six or seven days ago, Rodya, I was in despair at the way you were living, at your lodging, at your clothes, at your food. But now I see that it was all nonsense. In fact, as soon as you like, with your talent and your intelligence you will be able to earn as much as you like. For the time being, of course, you don't care about it, you are occupied with far more important things...."

“Dounia isn't here, mother?”

"No, Rodya. She is often out, and leaves me alone. Dmitri Prokofitch is kind enough to come and see me, and he always talks about you. He loves and esteems you, my friend. As for your sister, I do not complain of the little respect she shows me. She has her own character, as I have mine. It pleases her to keep her affairs to herself, and she is quite right. I have nothing to hide from my children. Of course, I am convinced that Dounia is very intelligent, and that, besides, she has a great affection for me and for you. But I don't know what will come of it all. I am sorry, too, that she cannot appreciate the good visit you have paid me. When she comes back, I shall say to her: "Your brother came to see me while you were away; where were you all the time?" You, Rodya, don't spoil me too much; come to see me when you can, but don't disturb yourself, and don't think about me. I shall get along very well without you. If I want anything, I'll send for you. I shall read your articles, and I'll hear all about you from everyone, and I shall know all about your affairs. I am not going to die, you know, Rodya. I shall live to see you a great man, and I shall be proud of you."

Suddenly Pulcheria Alexandrovna burst into tears.

Here I am again! Pay no attention to me, I am mad! Ah! Lord, but I am thinking of nothing! she exclaimed, rising suddenly.

— There's coffee, and I don't offer you any! You see what old people's selfishness is like. Right away, right away!

“It's not worth the trouble, Mother, I'm going to leave. I didn't come here for that. Listen to me, please.”

Pulcheria Alexandrovna timidly approached her son.

"Mamma, whatever happens, whatever you hear about me, will you love me as you do now?" he asked suddenly.

These words sprang spontaneously from the depths of his heart before he had had time to measure their scope.

“Rodia, Rodia, what is it? How can you ask me such a question? Who would dare to say anything bad about you? If someone dared to do that, I would refuse to listen and I would chase him away.”

"The purpose of my visit was to assure you that I have always loved you, and now I am very glad that we are alone, even glad that Dounyasha is not here," he continued with the same ardor; "perhaps you will be unhappy, but know that your son loves you now more than himself, and that you were wrong to doubt his tenderness. I shall never cease to love you. . . . Well, enough; I thought I ought, first of all, to give you this assurance. . . ."

Pulcheria Alexandrovna silently embraced her son, pressed him to her breast, and wept silently.

"I don't know what is the matter with you, Rodya," she said at last. "Until now I have simply thought that our presence was a burden to you; but now I see that some great misfortune is hanging over you, and that you are in great anxiety. I have guessed this, Rodya. Forgive me for talking to you about it; I can't help thinking about it and I can't sleep. Last night your sister was delirious; she kept on saying your name in her delirium. I heard something about it, but I don't know what. Since morning I have been in a state of suspense, waiting for you. I have a presentiment of something terrible. Rodya, Rodya, where are you going?" she asked, as he was about to leave. "You are going away, aren't you?"

— Yes.

"I guessed it! But I can go with you, if you must leave. Dounia will accompany us; she loves you, she loves you very much. If necessary, well, we will also take Sophia Semyonovna with us; you see? I am quite ready to accept her as a daughter. Dmitri Prokofitch will help us with our preparations for departure ... but ... where are you going then?"

— Good-bye, mother.

"What! to-day?" she cried, as if it were a question of eternal separation.

"I can't stay, I must absolutely leave you..."

— And I can't go with you?...

“No, but kneel down and pray to God for me. He may hear your prayer.”

— May he hear it! I will give you my blessing... Oh! Lord!

Yes, he was glad that his sister was not present at this interview. To pour out his affection freely, his tenderness needed the tête-à-tête, and any witness, even Dounia, would have embarrassed him. He fell at his mother's feet and kissed her hands. Pulcheria Alexandrovna and her son embraced, shedding tears; the first no longer asked any questions. She understood that the young man was passing through a terrible crisis, and that his fate would be decided in a moment.

"Rodia, my dear, my first-born," she said through her sobs, "you are now just as you were in your childhood; that is how you used to come to me with your caresses and kisses. In the past, when your father was alive, he and I had no other consolation in the midst of our misfortunes than your presence, and since I buried him, how many times have you and I not cried on his grave, embracing each other as we are now! If I have been crying for a long time, it is because my mother's heart had sinister forebodings. The evening we arrived in Petersburg, from our first meeting, your face told me everything, and today, when I opened the door to you, I thought, seeing you, that the fatal hour had come. Rodia, Rodia, you are not leaving right away?"

— No.

“Will you come again?”

— Yes… I will come.

“Rodia, don't be angry, I don't dare to question you; tell me only two words: are you going far from here?”

— Far away.

"Will you have a job there, a position?"

— I shall have what God sends me… pray for me only…

Raskolnikov wanted to leave, but she clung to him and looked him straight in the face with an expression of despair.

"Enough, mother," said the young man, who, a witness to this heartrending grief, deeply regretted having come.

"You're not leaving for good, are you? You're not going to set off right away? You'll come back tomorrow?"

— Yes, yes, goodbye.

He finally managed to escape.

The evening was warm, but not stifling; since morning the weather had cleared up. Raskolnikov quickly returned to his lodgings. He wanted to have everything finished before sunset. At the moment, any encounter would have been very unpleasant to him. On going up to his room, he noticed that Nastasia, who was then busy preparing tea, had interrupted her work and was following him with a curious eye.

"Is there anyone at home?" he wondered, and in spite of himself he thought of the odious Porfiry. But when he opened the door of his lodgings, he saw Dounia. The young girl was sitting on the sofa, pensive; she had probably been waiting for her brother for a long time. He stopped on the threshold. She gave a start of terror, rose quickly, and looked at him intently. There was an expression of immense desolation in Dounia's eyes. This one look convinced Raskolnikov that she knew everything.

“Shall I come to you or retreat?” he asked hesitantly.

“I spent the whole day waiting for you at Sophie Semyonovna's; we were expecting you there.”

Raskolnikov entered the room and sank down in exhaustion on a chair.

“I feel weak, Dounia; I am very tired, and especially at this moment I should need all my strength.”

He cast a defiant look at his sister.

— Where were you all night last night?

"I don't remember very well, you see, sister," he said. "I wanted to take a final decision, and several times I went to the Neva; that I remember. I meant to end it all there... but... I couldn't bring myself to it," he added in a low voice, looking at Dounia to see the effect of his words.

“Thank God! That is precisely what Sophie Semenovna and I feared! So you still believe in life, thank God!”

Raskolnikov smiled bitterly.

I did not believe it, but just now I went to our mother, and we embraced each other and wept; I am an unbeliever, and yet I asked her to pray for me. God knows how it is done, Dounia; I myself do not understand what I feel.

“You have been to see mother? You have spoken to her?” cried Dounia in alarm. “Can you have had the courage to tell her—”That?

“No, I didn't tell her… verbally; but she suspects something. She heard you talking in your sleep last night. I'm sure she has already guessed half of this secret. I may have been wrong to go to see her. I don't even know why I did it. I'm a low man, Dounia.”

— Yes, but a man ready to go to meet his expiation. You will go, won't you?

— Just now. To escape this disgrace, I wanted to drown myself, Dounia; but when I was about to throw myself into the water, I said to myself that a strong man should not be afraid of shame. Is that pride, Dounia?

— Yes, Rodia!

A sort of flash lit up his dull eyes; he seemed happy to think that he had retained his pride.

"Do you think, sister, that I was simply afraid of the water?" he asked with an unpleasant smile.

"Oh, Rodya, enough!" said the girl, hurt by this supposition.

They were both silent for ten minutes. Raskolnikov kept his eyes downcast; Dounia looked at him with an expression of suffering. Suddenly he got up:

— The hour is advancing, it is high time to leave. I am going to give myself up, but I do not know why I am doing this.

Great tears sprang to Dounia's eyes.

“You are weeping, my sister; but can you give me your hand?”

"Will you not atone for half your crime by offering yourself to expiation?" she cried, embracing her brother.

"My crime? What crime?" he said, suddenly getting angry. "That I killed a filthy, harmful vermin, an old usurer who was harmful to everyone, a vampire who sucked the blood of the poor? But such a murder should earn forgiveness for forty sins! I do not think about it and do not intend to erase it. Why do they all shout at me from all sides: "Crime! crime!" Now that I have decided to face this dishonor for nothing, now only the absurdity of my cowardly determination appears in all its light! It is simply out of baseness and powerlessness that I resolve to take this step, unless it is also out of interest, as this ... Porphyry advised me.

"Brother, brother, what are you saying? But you have shed blood!" cried Dunia, horrified.

"Well, what of it?" he continued, with increasing vehemence. "Everybody pours it out, and it has always flowed in streams over the earth; those who pour it out like champagne go up to the Capitol and are proclaimed benefactors of humanity. Look at things a little more closely before you judge them. I, too, wanted to do good to men; hundreds, thousands of good deeds would have atoned for this one piece of folly, and when I say folly I should rather say clumsiness, for the idea was not so foolish as it seems now: after failure, the best-laid plans seem idiotic. I wanted, by this folly, to create an independent position for myself, to secure my first steps in life, to provide myself with resources; then I would have taken my flight... But I failed, and therefore I am a wretch! If I had succeeded, they would have woven crowns for me, while now I am good for nothing but to be thrown to the dogs!"

— But no, it's not about that! My brother, what are you saying?

"It is true that I did not act according to the rules of aesthetics! I do not understand why it is more glorious to throw bombs at a besieged city than to murder someone with an axe! Fear of aesthetics is the first sign of impotence! I have never felt this more strongly than now, and I understand less than ever what my crime is! I have never been stronger, more convinced than I am now!"

His pale, haggard face suddenly flushed. But as he uttered this last exclamation, his eyes happened to meet Dounia's; she was looking at him with such sorrowful eyes that his exaltation suddenly vanished. He could not help thinking that, after all, he had made the unhappiness of these two poor women.

Dounia, my dear, if I am guilty, forgive me (though I do not deserve any forgiveness, if I am really guilty). Goodbye! Let us not argue together! It is time, high time to leave! Do not follow me, I beg you, I have another visit to make... Go immediately to find our mother and stay with her; I ask you in grace, it is the last prayer I address to you. Do not leave her; I left her very worried, and I fear that she will not resist her grief: she will either die or go mad. So watch over her! Razumikhin will not abandon you; I have spoken to him... Do not cry for me: although an assassin, I will try to be courageous and honest all my life. Perhaps you will hear of me one day. I will not dishonor you, you will see; I will prove again... Now, goodbye, he hurried to add, noticing a strange expression in Dounia's eyes as he made these promises.

— Why are you crying like that? Don't cry, we're not leaving each other forever!… Ah! yes! Wait, I forgot…

He went to the table and took from it a large book covered with dust, opened it, and took out a small watercolor painting on ivory. It was the portrait of the daughter of his landlady, the young woman he had loved. For a moment he looked at this expressive and suffering face, then he kissed the portrait and gave it back to Dounia.

— I have often talkedof thatWith her, with her alone, he said dreamily, I confided to her heart this project which was to have such a lamentable issue. Be at ease, he continued, addressing Dounia, she was revolted by it as much as you, and I am glad that she is dead.

Then, returning to the main object of his preoccupations:

"The main thing now," he said, "is to know if I have calculated correctly what I am going to do and if I am prepared to accept all the consequences. They say that this ordeal is necessary for me. Is that true? What moral strength will I have acquired when I leave the prison, broken by twenty years of suffering? Will it then be worth living? And I agree to bear the weight of such an existence! Oh! I felt that I was a coward this morning, when I was about to throw myself into the Neva!

At last they both went out. Dounia had been sustained during this painful interview only by her love for her brother. They parted in the street. After walking fifty paces, the girl turned round to look for the last time at Raskolnikov. When he reached the corner of the street, he too turned and looked back at her. Their eyes met, but, noticing that his sister was gazing at him, he made a gesture of impatience and even anger, to tell her to go on. Then he turned the corner and disappeared.

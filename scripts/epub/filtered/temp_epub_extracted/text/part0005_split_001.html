But you will not be able to hold out, and, if you remain alone, you will lose your reason; so will I, for that matter. Even now, you are like a madwoman. We must therefore walk together, follow the same road! Let's go!"

"Why? Why do you say that?" Sonia asked, strangely troubled by his words.

“Why? Because you cannot remain like this; that is why! You must finally reason seriously and see things as they are, instead of weeping like a child and putting everything on God! What will happen, I ask you, if tomorrow you are taken to the hospital? Catherine Ivanovna, almost insane and consumptive, will soon die; what will become of her children? Is not the loss of Poletchka certain?”

"What is to be done, then? What is to be done?" repeated Sonia, weeping and wringing her hands.

"What is to be done? We must cut the cable once and for all and go forward, come what may. You don't understand? You will understand later... Freedom and power, but above all power! To rule over all the trembling creatures, over the whole ant-hill!... That is the goal! Remember that! It is the testament I leave you. Perhaps I am speaking to you for the last time. If I don't come tomorrow, you will learn everything for yourself, and then remember my words. Later, in a few years, with the experience of life, you may perhaps understand what they meant. If I come tomorrow, I will tell you who killed Elizabeth. Goodbye!"

Sonia shuddered and looked at him with bewilderment.

“But do you know who killed her?” she asked, frozen with terror.

“I know it, and I will say it… To you, to you alone! I have chosen you. I will not come to ask your pardon, but simply to tell you that. I have chosen you for a long time. From the moment your father spoke to me of you, even during Elizabeth's lifetime, the idea came to me. Adieu. Do not give me your hand. Until tomorrow!”

He went out, leaving Sonia with the impression of a madman; but she herself was like a madwoman, and she felt it. Her head was spinning. "Lord! How does he know who killed Elizabeth? What did those words mean? This is strange!" Yet she had not the slightest suspicion of the truth... "Oh! he must be terribly unhappy!... He left his mother and sister. Why? What happened? And what are his intentions? What did he say to me? He kissed my foot and he said... he said (yes, he expressed himself exactly like that) that he could no longer live without me... Oh Lord!"

Behind the locked door was a room that had been unoccupied for a long time, which was part of the lodgings of Gertrude Karlovna Resslich. This room was for rent, as indicated by a sign placed outside the main door and papers pasted on the windows overlooking the canal. Sonia knew that no one lived there. But, during the entire previous scene, Mr. Svidrigailov, hidden behind the door, had not ceased to lend an attentive ear to the conversation. When Raskolnikov left, the tenant of Madame Resslich thought for a moment, then he quietly went back into his room which was next to the empty room, took a chair and brought it right up to the door. What he had just heard had interested him to the highest degree; so he brought this chair so that he could listen the next time, without being forced to stand on his feet for an hour.

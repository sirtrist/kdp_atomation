Here is the memory that this scene left in Raskolnikov's mind:

The noise that was being made in the next room suddenly increased, and the door opened.

"What is it?" cried Porfiry Petrovitch angrily.

“I warned you…”

There was no answer, but the cause of the uproar could be partly guessed: someone wanted to enter the magistrate's office, and they were trying to prevent him.

"What is there then?" repeated Porphyre, anxiously.

"It is the accused Nicolas who has been brought in," said a voice.

"I don't need him! I don't want to see him! Take him away! Wait a minute! How did they bring him here? What a mess!" Porphyre roared, rushing to the door.

"But it was he who . . . ," the same voice continued, and then suddenly stopped.

For two seconds there was the sound of a struggle between two men; then one of them pushed the other back with force and, suddenly, burst into the room.

The newcomer had a very strange appearance. He looked straight ahead, but did not seem to see anyone. Resolution was written in his sparkling eyes, and, at the same time, his face was as livid as that of a condemned man being led to the scaffold. His lips, all white, trembled slightly.

He was a man still quite young, thin, of medium height, and dressed like a workman; he had short-cropped hair; his features were fine and dry. The man he had just pushed back rushed after him into the room and seized him by the shoulder: it was a gendarme; but Nicolas succeeded once again in freeing himself.

Several curious people gathered on the threshold. Some of them were very eager to enter. All this had happened in much less time than we have taken to tell it.

"Go away, it's too early yet! Wait until you're called!... Why have they brought him in so early?" Porfiry Petrovitch grumbled, as irritated as he was surprised. But suddenly Nikolay fell on his knees.

“What are you doing?” cried the examining magistrate, more and more astonished.

“Pardon! I am guilty! I am the murderer!” said Nicolas suddenly, in a voice loud enough, in spite of the emotion that choked him.

During ten seconds there was a silence so profound that it seemed as if all the assistants had fallen into catalepsy; the gendarme no longer tried to retake his prisoner, and mechanically turned towards the door, where he remained motionless.

"What are you saying?" cried Porfiry Petrovich, when his amazement allowed him to speak.

“I am… the murderer,” Nicolas repeated after a moment of silence.

— How… you… How… Who did you murder?

The examining magistrate was visibly disconcerted.

Nicolas waited a moment longer before answering.

"I have... murdered... with an axe, Alena Ivanovna and her sister Elizabeth Ivanovna. I was out of my mind," he added suddenly, and then he fell silent, but remained kneeling.

After hearing this reply, Porfiry Petrovitch seemed to reflect deeply; then, with a violent gesture, he invited the witnesses to withdraw. They obeyed at once, and the door closed.

Raskolnikov, standing in a corner, was looking at Nicolas with a strange expression. For a few moments the magistrate's eyes went from the visitor to the prisoner and back again.vice versaIn the end, he turned to Nicolas with a kind of vehemence:

"Wait until you are questioned before telling me you were out of your mind!" he said in a voice almost irritated. "I haven't asked you that yet... Speak now: did you kill?"

"I am the murderer," said Nicolas. "I confess."

— E-eh! With what did you kill?

“With an axe. I had brought it for that purpose.”

“Oh! how he hurries! Alone?”

Nicolas did not understand the question.

“Did you have any accomplices?”

“No. Mitka is innocent, he had no part in the crime.”

“Don't be in such a hurry to clear Mitka; did I ask you anything about him?… But how is it that the dvorniks saw you both running down the stairs?”

"I ran after Mitka on purpose," answered Nicholas, "it was a feint to divert suspicion."

"Well, that's enough, that's enough!" cried Porfiry, angrily; "he's not telling the truth!" he muttered afterwards, as if to himself, and suddenly his eyes met Raskolnikov, whose presence he had evidently forgotten during his conversation with Nikolay. On seeing his visitor, the examining magistrate seemed to be somewhat disconcerted. He immediately approached him.

"Rodion Romanovitch, batuchka! Excuse me... I beg you... you have nothing more to do here... I myself... you see what a surprise!... I beg you..."

He had taken the young man by the arm and was showing him the door.

"You don't seem to have expected that," observed Raskolnikov.

Naturally, what had just happened was still an enigma to him, but he had regained a good deal of his assurance.

"But you didn't expect that either, batuchka. Look how your hand is shaking! Ha! ha!"

"You are trembling too, Porfiry Petrovitch."

— It is true; I did not expect that!…

They were already on the threshold of the door. The examining magistrate was anxious to be rid of his visitor.

"So you won't show me the little surprise?" he asked abruptly.

"He can scarcely speak, and he is already making fun of me! Ha! ha! you are a caustic man! Well, good-bye!"

"It will be as God wills!" Porphyre mumbled with a forced smile.

Crossing the chancellery, Raskolnikov noticed that several of the employees were staring at him fixedly. In the antechamber, he recognized, in the middle of the crowd, the two dvorniks of that house, those to whom he had proposed, the other evening, to take him to the police commissioner. They seemed to be waiting for something. But as soon as he had reached the square, he heard again behind him the voice of Porphyre Petrovitch. He turned around and saw the investigating magistrate panting to catch up with him.

“Just a word, Rodion Romanovitch; it will be as God wills with this affair, but for the sake of form I shall have to ask you some questions… so we shall see each other again, certainly!”

And Porphyre stopped, smiling at the young man.

"Certainly!" he said a second time.

One could have supposed that he would have wanted to say something more, but he added nothing.

“Pardon me for my behavior yesterday, Porfiry Petrovich… I was a little sharp,” began Raskolnikov, who had regained all his composure and even felt an irresistible urge to tease the magistrate.

"Never mind," Porphyry said in a tone almost cheerful. "I myself... I have a very disagreeable character, I confess. But we shall see each other again. If God wills, we shall see each other often!"

"And we shall make our acquaintance definitively," said Raskolnikov.

And we shall make a definite acquaintance, repeated Porfiry Petrovitch, as if in echo, and, winking, he looked very seriously at his interlocutor.

— Now you are going to a festive dinner?

— At a funeral.

— Ah! that is right! Take care of your health.

"I don't know what wishes to make for you," answered Raskolnikov; he was already beginning to go down the stairs, but suddenly he turned to Porfiry:

"I should like to wish you more success than you have had today; yet see how comic your functions are!"

At these words, the investigating magistrate, who was already preparing to return to his apartment, pricked up his ears.

“What is there funny about them?” he asked.

"But how is it? Here is this poor Mikolka; how you must have tormented him, harassed him, to extort an avowal from him! Day and night, no doubt, you have repeated to him in every tone: "You are the murderer, you are the murderer." You have persecuted him without respite according to your psychological method. Now, now that he acknowledges himself guilty, you begin to harass him again by singing him another tune: "You are lying, you are not the murderer, you cannot be, you are not telling the truth." Well, after that, have I not the right to find your functions comical?"

— Ha! ha! ha! So you noticed that just now I told Nicolas he was not telling the truth?

— How could I not have noticed it?

— He! he! You have a subtle mind, nothing escapes you. And, moreover, you cultivate the joke, you have the humorous string, he! he! It was, they say, the distinctive trait of our writer Gogol?

— Yes, by Gogol.

— Indeed, by Gogol… It was a pleasure to meet you.

— I look forward to seeing you again…

The young man went straight home. When he arrived at his lodgings, he threw himself on his sofa, and for a quarter of an hour tried to put a little order into his ideas, which were very confused. He did not even try to explain Nicolas's conduct, feeling that there was a mystery there which, for the moment, he would seek in vain to unravel. Besides, he did not deceive himself as to the probable consequences of the incident: the worker's confession would not be long in being recognized as false, and then suspicion would fall again on him, Raskolnikoff. But, in the meantime, he was free and he must take his measures in anticipation of the danger which he judged imminent.

Raskolnikov was sitting on the sofa, his elbows on his knees and his head in his hands. A nervous trembling continued to agitate his whole body. At last he got up, took his cap, and after thinking for a moment, went to the door.

He told himself that, at least for today, he had nothing to fear. Suddenly he felt a kind of joy: the idea came to him to go to Catherine Ivanovna as soon as possible. Of course, it was too late to go to the funeral, but he would arrive in time for dinner, and there he would see Sonia.

He stopped, thought, and a sickly smile appeared on his lips.

"Today! Today!" he repeated; "yes, today! It must be done!"

As he was about to open the door, it opened by itself. He stepped back in terror as the enigmatic figure of the previous night appeared, the manemerged from under the ground.

The visitor stopped on the threshold, and after looking silently at Raskolnikov, stepped into the room. He was dressed exactly as he had been the day before, but his face was no longer the same. He seemed greatly distressed, and heaved deep sighs.

“What do you want?” asked Raskolnikov, pale as death.

The man did not answer, and suddenly bowed almost to the ground. At least, he touched the floor with the ring he wore on his right hand.

“Who are you?” cried Raskolnikov.

“I beg your pardon,” said the man in a low voice.

— Of what?

— Of my evil thoughts.

They looked at each other.

"I was angry. When you came the other day, perhaps your mind was troubled by drink, you asked about the blood and asked the dvorniks to take you to the police station. I saw with regret that they did not take your words into account, taking you for a drunk man. This upset me so much that I could not sleep. But I remembered your address, and yesterday I came here...

“It was you who came?” interrupted Raskolnikov.

Light was beginning to dawn in his mind.

— Yes. I insulted you.

— You were in that house, then?

— Yes, I was under the carriage entrance when you came. Have you forgotten? I have lived there for a long time, I am a furrier…

Raskolnikov suddenly recalled the whole scene of the day before: in fact, apart from the dvorniks, there were still several people under the gateway, men and women. Someone had proposed to take him immediately to the police station. He could not remember the face of the one who had given this advice, and now he did not even recognize him, but he remembered having answered him something, having turned to him.

Thus, the most simple explanation of the terrifying mystery of the previous day was revealed. And, under the impression of the anxiety caused by such an insignificant circumstance, he had almost lost himself! This man had not been able to tell anything, except that Raskolnikov had presented himself to rent the old woman's apartment and had asked about the blood. "Therefore," thought the young man, "if no new charges arise (and they will not arise, I am sure!), what can be done to me? Even if I were arrested, how could my guilt be definitively established?"

Another conclusion Raskolnikov drew from his visitor's words was that Porfiry had only just learned of his visit to the victim's apartment.

The first time I went there he was out. An hour later I returned and was not received; finally, the third time, I was admitted. "No precise charges, nothing but evidence that could be interpreted in two ways!" thought Raskolnikov, reviving with hope, and he went out of the room.

"Now we can still fight," he said to himself with a smile of anger, as he went down the stairs. It was at himself that he was angry; he thought with humiliation of his "pusillanimity."

He had expected to find the apartment exactly as he had left it; perhaps he even imagined that the corpses were still lying on the floor. Now, to his great surprise, the walls were bare, the rooms were unfurnished! He went to the window and sat down on the window sill.

There were only two workmen, two young men, one of whom was a little older than the other. They were replacing the old yellow wallpaper, which was quite worn out, with white paper strewn with small violet flowers. This circumstance — we do not know why — greatly displeased Raskolnikov. He looked at the new paper with anger, as if all these changes had annoyed him.

The upholsterers were getting ready to go home. They paid hardly any attention to the visitor and continued their conversation.

Raskolnikov got up and went into the other room, which had formerly contained the chest, the bed, and the chest of drawers. This room, now empty of furniture, seemed to him extremely small. The wallpaper had not been changed; one could still make out in the corner the place occupied by the cupboard with the holy pictures. After satisfying his curiosity, Raskolnikov returned to sit on the window-sill. The older of the two workmen glanced at him sideways, and suddenly addressing him:

“What are you doing there?” he asked.

Instead of answering, Raskolnikov got up, went into the vestibule, and began to pull the bell. It was the same bell, the same tinny sound! He rang a second, a third time, listening and recalling his memories. The terrible impression he had felt the other day at the old woman's door came back to him with increasing clarity and vividness; he shivered at each ring and took a greater and greater pleasure in it.

"But what do you want? Who are you?" cried the workman, advancing towards him.

Raskolnikov then returned to the apartment.

"I want to rent a room, and I came to see this one," he said.

“It is not at night that one visits apartments, and, besides, you should have been accompanied by the dvornik.”

"They have washed the floor; they are going to paint it," continued Raskolnikov. "There's no blood?"

— What, blood?

"But the old woman and her sister were murdered. There was a great pool of blood there."

“What kind of man are you?” cried the worker, alarmed.

— Me?

— Yes.

"Do you want to know?"... Let us go together to the police station, and there I will tell you.

The two upholsterers stared at him in amazement.

"It is time for us to go. Let's go, Alechka. We must close," said the older one to his companion.

"Well, let's go!" Raskolnikov repeated in an indifferent tone. He went out first and, preceding the two men, slowly descended the stairs.

"Hallo! dvornik!" he cried, when he reached the great gate.

Several people were standing at the entrance of the house, looking at the passers-by. There were the two dvorniks, a peasant woman, a tradesman in a dressing-gown, and some other individuals. Raskolnikov went straight up to them.

“What do you want?” asked one of the dvorniks.

“Did you go to the police station?”

— I have just been there. What do you want?

— Are they still there?

— Yes.

— Is the commissioner's deputy here, too?

— He was here just now. What do you want?

Raskolnikov did not answer, and remained thoughtful.

“He came to visit the lodgings,” said the older of the two workers, approaching.

— What kind of lodging?

— The one where we work. "Why has the blood been washed away?" he said. "A murder has been committed here, and I come to rent the apartment." He began to ring the bell, and he almost broke the bell-cord. "Let us go to the police station," he added, "there I will tell all."

The dvornik, puzzled, examined Raskolnikov with a frown.

"Who are you?" he asked, raising his voice in a threatening tone.

— I am Rodion Romanovitch Raskolnikov, a former student, and I live near here, in the next lane, in the house of Chill, apartment No. 11. Ask the dvornik… he knows me.

Raskolnikov gave all these details with the most indifferent and tranquil air; he kept his eyes fixed on the street, and did not once turn his head towards his interlocutor.

“What are you doing in this apartment?”

— I have come to visit him.

— What do you have to do with this?

“Shouldn't we take him and bring him to the police station?” the bourgeois suddenly suggested.

Raskolnikov looked at him attentively over his shoulder.

"Let's go," he said with indifference.

"Yes, we must take him to the police station!" the bourgeois repeated with more assurance. "For him to have gone there, he must have something on his conscience..."

"God knows whether he is drunk or not," muttered the workman.

“But what do you want?” cried the dvornik again, beginning to get seriously angry. “Why do you come here to bother us?”

"You are afraid to go to the police station?" Raskolnikov sneered.

— Why should I be afraid? Come, you are boring us.

"He is a thief!" cried the peasant woman.

"What's the use of arguing with him?" cried the other dvornik, a huge moujik in a loose smock, with a bunch of keys hanging from his belt. "He's a thief, for sure! Come, be off, and be quick about it!"

And seizing Raskolnikov by the shoulder, he flung him into the street. The young man nearly fell to the ground, but did not fall. When he had regained his balance, he looked silently at all the spectators, then walked away.

"He's a queer man," said the workman.

"People have become funny nowadays," said the peasant woman.

"Anyway, he should have been taken to the police station," added the bourgeois.

"Shall I go or not?" thought Raskolnikov, stopping in the middle of a crossroad and looking around him, as if he expected someone to give him advice. But his question received no answer; everything was deaf and lifeless, like the stones he was walking on. Suddenly, two hundred paces from him, at the end of a street, he could make out, through the darkness, a crowd from which came cries and animated words. The crowd was surrounding a carriage. A faint light shone in the middle of the pavement. "What's going on there?" Raskolnikov turned right and went to join the crowd. He seemed to want to cling to the slightest incident, and this puerile disposition made him smile, for his mind was made up, and he told himself that, in an instant, "he would finish with all this."

Until ten o'clock in the evening, Arkady Ivanovich Svidrigailov ran through the dives and taverns. Having found Katya in one of these places, he paid for her drinks as well as for the organ player, the waiters and two young clerks towards whom he had been drawn by a strange sympathy: he had noticed that these two young men had crooked noses, and that the nose of one was turned to the right, while that of the other looked to the left. Finally, he allowed himself to be dragged by them into a "pleasure garden" where he paid their entrance fee. This establishment, decorated with the name of Waux-Hall, was in fact only a low-class singing café. The clerks met some "colleagues" there and quarreled with them. It was almost a fight. Svidrigailov was chosen as arbitrator. After listening for a quarter of an hour to the confused complaints of the parties involved, he thought he understood that one of the clerks had stolen something and sold it to a Jew, but did not want to share the proceeds of this commercial operation with his comrades. In the end, it turned out that the stolen object was a tea spoon belonging to the Waux-Hall. It was recognized by the people of the establishment, and the matter threatened to take a serious turn if Svidrigailov had not disinterested the complainants. He then got up and left the garden. It was then about ten o'clock.

During the whole evening he had not drunk a drop of wine; at the Waux-Hall he had only asked for tea, and that because it was the custom to order something. The heat was stifling, and black clouds were gathering in the sky. Towards ten o'clock a violent storm broke out. Svidrigailov returned home drenched to the skin. He locked himself in his room, opened his bureau, took out all his money, and tore up two or three papers. After putting his money in his pocket, he thought of changing his clothes; but as it was still raining, he decided that it was not worth while, took his hat, and went out without locking his door. He went straight to Sonia's, whom he found at home.

The girl was not alone; she had four little children of the Kapernaoumoff family around her. Sophie Semyonovna was giving them tea. She received the visitor respectfully, looked with surprise at his wet clothes, but said not a word. At the sight of a stranger, all the children immediately ran away, seized with indescribable fear.

Svidrigaïlov sat down at the table and invited Sonia to sit down beside him. She prepared herself timidly to listen to what he had to say.

"Sophie Semyonovna," he began, "I may be going to America, and as, in all probability, we shall see each other for the last time, I have come to settle some affairs. Well, have you been to that lady today?" He knew what she had been told, and it was unnecessary for her to repeat it. (Sofia made a movement and blushed.) "Such people have their prejudices. As for your sisters and your brother, their fate is assured; the money I intended for each of them has been placed in safe hands. Here are the receipts; take them, just in case. Now, here are three five-per-cent bonds for you personally, amounting to three thousand roubles. I wish this to remain between ourselves, and that no one else should know of it. This money is necessary for you, Sophie Semyonovna, for you cannot go on living like this."

"You have been so kind to the orphans, to the dead woman, and to me," Sonia stammered, "and if I have not thanked you as I should, don't think…"

— Come, enough, enough!

"As for this money, Arcade Ivanovitch, I am very grateful to you, but I do not need it now. Having only myself to feed, I will always be able to manage. Do not accuse me of ingratitude if I refuse your offer. Since you are so charitable, this money ..."

“Take it, Sofya Semyonovna, and please don't object to it, I haven't time to listen to your objections. Rodion Romanovitch has only two alternatives: either to put a bullet through his head or to go to Siberia…

At these words Sonia began to tremble and looked at her interlocutor with dismay.

“Don't worry,” Svidrigailov continued. “I learned everything from his own mouth, and I'm not a gossip; I won't tell anyone. You were right to advise him to turn himself in. It's by far the most advantageous course he can take. Well, when he goes to Siberia, you'll accompany him, won't you? In that case, you'll need money; you'll need it for him, understand? The sum I'm offering you, I'm giving it to him through you. Besides, you promised Amalia Ivanovna to pay what she's owed. Why do you always take on such obligations so lightly, Sofya Semyonovna? The debtor of that German woman was not you, but Katerina Ivanovna; you should have sent the German woman to the devil. One needs more calculation in life… Well, if tomorrow or the day after tomorrow someone asks you about me, don't mention my visit and don't tell anyone that I gave you money. And now goodbye.” (He stood up.) “Give my regards to Rodion Romanovich. By the way: in the meantime, you'd do well to entrust the money to Mr. Razumikhin. You probably know Mr. Razumikhin? He's a good fellow. Bring it to him tomorrow or… when you have the opportunity. But until then, try not to let anyone take it from you.”

Sonia had also risen and was looking at the visitor with an anxious expression. She wanted very much to say something, to ask some question, but she was timid and did not know where to begin.

"So you are... so you are going to set off in such weather?"

"When one is going to America, does one worry about the rain? Good-bye, dear Sophie Semyonovna! Live and live long, you are useful to others. By the way, give my compliments to Mr. Razumihin. Tell him that Arkady Ivanovitch Svidrigaïlov salutes him. Don't forget."

When he left her, Sonia was oppressed by a vague feeling of fear.

The same evening, Svidrigaïloff paid another visit, very singular and very unexpected. The rain was still falling. At eleven twenty, he presented himself, all wet, at the parents of his future wife, who occupied a small apartment in Vasili-Ostroff. He had great difficulty getting in, and his arrival at such an inopportune hour caused extreme stupefaction at first. At first it was thought to be a prank of a drunk man, but this impression lasted only an instant; for, when he wanted to, Arcade Ivanovitch had the most seductive manners. The intelligent mother rolled the invalid father's armchair up to him and immediately began the conversation with roundabout questions. This woman never went straight to the point: if she wanted to know, for example, when Arcade Ivanovitch would like the marriage to be celebrated, she would begin by curiously questioning him about Paris, about thehigh lifeParisian, to bring him back gradually to Vasili-Ostroff.

This little trick had been used before, and had always succeeded; but this time Svidrigaïloff was more impatient than usual, and asked to see his bride, though he had been told that she was already in bed. Of course, they hastened to comply with his request. Arkady Ivanovitch told the young girl that an urgent matter obliged him to leave Petersburg for a time, and that he had brought her fifteen thousand roubles, which he begged her to accept as a trifle which he had long intended to give her before their marriage. There was not much connection between this present and his departure; nor did it seem to require a visit in the middle of the night, in the pouring rain. Nevertheless, however suspicious they might seem, these explanations were perfectly well received; the parents scarcely showed any surprise at such strange proceedings; they were very sparing of questions and exclamations, but they were profuse in the warmest expressions of gratitude, to which the intelligent mother added her tears. Svidrigaïloff rose, kissed his betrothed, patted her cheek affectionately, and assured her that he would soon be back. The little girl looked at him with an inquiring air; there was more than mere childish curiosity in her eyes. Arkady Ivanovitch noticed this look; he kissed his betrothed again, and went out, thinking with real vexation that his present would be sure to be locked up by the most intelligent of mothers.

At midnight he was returning to town by the *** bridge. The rain had stopped, but the wind was blowing hard. For nearly half an hour Svidrigailov paced the pavement of the huge *** Prospect, as if looking for something. Not long before, he had noticed, on the right side of the Prospect, a hotel which, as far as he could remember, was called the Hotel d'Andrinople. At last he found it. It was a long wooden building, in which, despite the late hour, lights were still burning. He went in and asked for a room from a ragged servant he met in the corridor. After a glance at Svidrigailov, the servant led him to a small room at the very end of the corridor, under the stairs. It was the only room available.

"Do you have any tea?" asked Svidrigaïlov.

— We can make you some.

— What else is there?

— Veal, brandy, hors-d'œuvres.

— Bring me some veal and tea.

“Do you want anything else?” asked the valet with a sort of hesitation.

— No.

The man in rags walked away very disappointed.

"This must be a decent place," thought Svidrigaïlov; "and, besides, I must look like a man coming from a music hall, who has had an adventure on the way. I should like to know what sort of people live here."

He lit the candle and began to examine the room more closely. It was very narrow and so low that a man of Svidrigailov's height could scarcely stand upright in it. The furniture consisted of a very dirty bed, a table covered with oilcloth, and a chair. The shabby wallpaper was so covered with dust that it was difficult to make out its original color. The staircase cut obliquely across the ceiling, which gave the room the appearance of a garret. Svidrigailov put the candle on the table, sat down on the bed, and became thoughtful. But the incessant noise of voices which came from the next room finally attracted his attention. He got up, took his candle, and went to look through a crack in the wall.

In a room a little larger than his own, he saw two men, one standing and the other sitting on a chair. The first, in his shirt sleeves, was red-faced and had curly hair. He was reproaching his companion, with tears in his voice: "You had no position, you were in the depths of misery; I pulled you out of the mire, and it only depends on me to throw you back into it." The friend to whom these words were addressed had the appearance of a man who wants to sneeze but cannot. From time to time he cast a dazed glance at the speaker: evidently he did not understand a word of what was being said to him, perhaps he did not even hear it. On the table, where the candle was just going out, there was a nearly empty carafe of brandy, glasses of different sizes, bread, cucumbers, and a tea service. After attentively considering this scene, Svidrigailov left his post of observation and returned to sit on the bed.

When the boy brought the tea and the veal, he could not help asking again if there was anything else he could do for him. Receiving a negative answer, he finally withdrew. Svidrigailov hurriedly drank a glass of tea to warm himself, but he could not eat. The fever that was beginning to agitate him took away his appetite. He took off his overcoat and jacket, wrapped himself in the bedspread, and lay down. He was annoyed. "It would be better for me to be healthy this time," he thought with a smile. The atmosphere was stifling, the candle gave off a dim light, the wind was roaring outside, a mouse could be heard rustling in a corner, and, in general, the whole room was filled with the smell of mice and leather. Lying on the bed, Svidrigailov was more dreaming than thinking, his thoughts were confused, he wanted to fix his imagination on something. "There must be a garden under the window, the trees are swaying in the wind. How I hate the sound of trees at night, in a storm and in the dark!" He remembered that he had had the same unpleasant impression when he passed by the Petrovsky Park earlier. Then he thought about the Little Neva, and he again felt the chill that had seized him earlier when he stood on the bridge and looked at the river. "I have never liked water in my life, even in landscapes," he thought, and suddenly a strange thought made him smile: "It seems to me that now I should not care about aesthetics and comfort, but here I am becoming as demanding as an animal that always takes care to choose its place... in such cases. If I had gone to Petrovsky Ostroff earlier? Apparently I was afraid of the cold and the darkness, hee hee! I need pleasant sensations!... But why not put out the candle?" (He blew it out.) "My neighbors are asleep," he added, not seeing any light in the crack in the wall. "Now, Marfa Petrovna, your visit would be appropriate. It's dark, the place is suitable, the situation is exceptional. And, of course, you will not come..."

Sleep continued to elude him. Gradually the image of Dounia rose before him, and a sudden tremor shook his limbs at the memory of the scene he had had with the girl a few hours before. "No, let's not think about that. Strange, I've never hated anyone very much, I've never even felt a strong desire to take revenge on anyone, that's a bad sign, a bad sign! I've never been quarrelsome or violent either - that's another bad sign! But what promises I made her just now! She would have led me far ... " He fell silent and clenched his teeth. His imagination showed him Dounia again, exactly as she was when, after dropping the revolver, now incapable of resistance, she fixed her terrified gaze on him. He remembered how sorry he had felt for her at that moment, how his heart had been oppressed ... "To hell with it! These thoughts again! Let's not think about all this. "

He was already falling asleep, his feverish trembling had ceased; suddenly it seemed to him that something was running along his arm and leg under the blanket. He shuddered: "The devil! It must be a mouse," he thought. "I left the veal on the table..." Fearing that he would catch cold, he did not want to uncover himself or get up, but suddenly a new unpleasant sensation brushed his foot. He threw off the blanket and lit the candle, then, shivering, he bent over the bed and examined it without finding anything. He shook the blanket, and suddenly a mouse jumped onto the sheet. He immediately tried to catch it, but, while still on the bed, it described zigzags in different directions and slipped under the fingers that were trying to seize it; suddenly the mouse hid under the pillow. Svidrigailov threw the pillow on the floor, but at the same instant he felt that something had jumped on him and was running over his body under his shirt. A nervous trembling seized him, and he woke up. The room was dark, he was lying on the bed, wrapped, as before, in the blanket; the wind was still roaring outside: "It's creepy!" he said to himself angrily.

He rose and sat on the edge of the bed, with his back to the window. "It is better not to sleep," he decided. A cold, damp draught blew in from the window; without moving from his place, Svidrigaïlov drew the blanket over him and wrapped himself in it. He did not light the candle. He did not think of anything and did not want to think, but dreams, incoherent ideas, passed through his brain. He was in a sort of half-sleep. Was it the cold, the darkness, the dampness, or the wind that was blowing in the trees, that had given his reveries a fantastic turn, and made him see flowers everywhere? He seemed to be looking at a bright landscape; it was Trinity Day, and the weather was fine. In the midst of flower-beds stood a charming little English cottage; creepers twined round the porch, and pots of rare flowers stood on either side of the steps, covered with a rich carpet. In the windows were vases of white hyacinths, which drooped on their dark green stalks and filled the air with a heavy fragrance. These hyacinths particularly attracted Svidrigaïlov's attention, and he felt a pang at the thought of breaking off one of them. He went up the steps and entered a large, lofty room; there were flowers everywhere, on the windows, on the table, on the sofa, and on the floor. The floor was strewn with freshly-cut grass, which exhaled a delicious fragrance. The windows were open, and a fresh, cool breeze blew into the room. The birds were chirping under the windows. But in the middle of the room, on a table covered with a white satin cloth, stood a coffin. Around it were wreaths of flowers, and it was covered with white silk and muslin. In this coffin lay a young girl in a white muslin dress, her hands crossed on her breast, as though carved out of marble. Her fair hair was disordered and wet, and a wreath of roses lay on her head. The severe and already rigid profile of her face seemed as though chiselled out of marble, but the smile on her pale lips was full of a mournful, unchildlike sadness. Svidrigaïlov knew this girl; there were no holy images, no burning candles, no prayers beside the coffin. The dead girl had been a suicide, had drowned herself. At the age of fourteen she had been seduced, and her childish conscience had been so crushed, her angelic purity so defiled, that she had thrown herself into the water and drowned herself on a cold, dark, damp night, at the time of the thaw.

Svidrigaïloff woke up, got out of bed, and went to the window. After groping for the latch, he opened the window, exposing his face and chest, barely protected by his shirt, to the icy wind that rushed into the narrow room. Under the window, there must have been a garden, probably a pleasure garden; there, no doubt, during the day, they sang songs and served tea on small tables. But now everything was plunged into darkness, and objects were only indicated to the eye by barely distinguishable black spots. For five minutes, Svidrigaïloff, leaning on the windowsill, looked down into the darkness. In the middle of the night, two cannon shots rang out.

"Ah! this is a signal! The Neva is rising," he thought; "this morning the lower parts of the city will be flooded, the rats will be drowned in the cellars; the tenants of the ground floors, dripping with water, grumbling, will be carrying out their knick-knacks in the midst of the rain and wind; they will have to take them up to the upper floors... But what time is it?" At the very moment he was asking himself this question, a clock in the neighborhood struck three.

"Why wait? In an hour it will be daylight. I will go at once to the Petrograd Island." He closed the window, lighted a candle, and dressed; then, with the candle in his hand, he left the room to wake the waiter, pay his bill, and leave the hotel.

A thick fog covered the city. Svidrigailov walked in the direction of the Little Neva. As he walked on the slippery wooden pavement, he imagined the Petrovsky Island with its small paths, lawns, trees, and thickets ... Not a pedestrian, not a cab in the whole length of the perspective. The little yellow houses, with their shutters closed, looked dirty and sad. The cold and dampness began to give the morning stroller a chill. From time to time, when he saw the sign of a shop, he read it mechanically.

When he reached the end of the wooden pavement, opposite the big stone house, he saw a very ugly dog crossing the road, its tail between its legs.

V.

When, the next day, at exactly eleven o'clock, Raskolnikov presented himself at the examining magistrate's office, he was surprised to have to wait in the anteroom for quite a long time. According to what he had presumed, he should have been received immediately; but at least ten minutes passed before he was able to see Porfiry Petrovich. In the entrance room, where he waited first, people came and went without seeming to pay the slightest attention to him. In the next room, which resembled a chancellery, some scribes were working, and it was evident that none of them had even the slightest idea of what Raskolnikov might be.

The young man cast a defiant glance around him: was there not some spy, some mysterious Argus, charged with watching him and, if necessary, preventing his escape? But he discovered nothing of the sort: the scribes were all absorbed in their work, and the others paid no attention to him. The visitor began to feel reassured. "If, in fact," he thought, "this mysterious person of yesterday, this spectre, risen from the underworld, knew everything and had seen everything, well, would they let me stand here like this? And even would they not have arrested me already instead of waiting for me to come here of my own free will? Therefore, either this man has not yet made any revelations against me, or... or he simply knows nothing and has seen nothing (besides, how could he have seen?), therefore I was hallucinating, and everything that happened to me yesterday was only an illusion of my sick imagination." He found this explanation more and more plausible, which had already presented itself to his mind the day before when he was most anxious.

While he was thinking about all this and preparing himself for a new struggle, Raskolnikov suddenly noticed that he was trembling, and he was even indignant at the thought that it was fear of an interview with the hateful Porfiry Petrovich that made him tremble. The most terrible thing for him was to find himself again in the presence of this man: he hated him beyond measure and he even feared betraying himself by his hatred. His indignation was so strong that it stopped his trembling altogether; he prepared himself to enter with a cold and confident air, promised himself to speak as little as possible, to be always on the alert, and finally to master his irascible nature at all costs. In the meantime, he was introduced to Porfiry Petrovich.

He was alone in his office. This room, of medium size, contained a large table facing a sofa covered in oilcloth, a desk, a cupboard in a corner and a few chairs; all this furniture, supplied by the State, was made of yellow wood. In the wall, or rather the partition at the back, there was a closed door, which gave the impression that there must be other rooms behind the partition.

As soon as Porfiry Petrovitch saw Raskolnikov enter his office, he went to close the door through which the young man had entered, and they were left alone. The examining magistrate received his visitor with the most apparently cheerful and affable welcome; it was only after a few minutes that Raskolnikov noticed the magistrate's slightly embarrassed manner: it seemed as though he had been disturbed in the midst of some clandestine occupation.

"Ah! Very-respectable! You are here... in our neighborhood..." began Porphyry, extending both hands to him. "Come, sit down, batuchka! But perhaps you don't like being called very-respectable and... batuchka, just like that? Don't take this as familiarity, please... Here, on the sofa."

Raskolnikov sat down, without taking his eyes off the examining magistrate.

"These words, 'in our neighborhood,' these excuses for his familiarity, this French expression 'tout court,' what do they all mean? He held out both hands to me without giving me either one, and drew them back in time," thought Raskolnikov, who was now on his guard. They looked at each other, but as soon as their eyes met, they turned them away with the rapidity of lightning.

“I came to bring you this paper… about the watch… Here it is. Is it all right, or shall I write another letter?”

What? What paper? Yes, yes… don't worry, it's fine, Porphyry replied with a kind of haste, who said these words before even examining the paper, then, when he had taken a quick look at it:

"Yes, that's very good, that's all that is needed," he continued, still speaking very fast, and he laid the paper on the table. A minute later he put it in his desk, while talking of something else.

“You expressed yesterday, I believe, the desire to question me… in due form… about my relations with the… victim?” Raskolnikov resumed.

« Come, why did I say:it seems to me"What does it matter?" thought the young man suddenly. "What am I worrying about?" he added mentally almost immediately.

The mere fact that he was in Porphyry's presence, with whom he had barely exchanged two words, had caused his mistrust to reach absurd proportions; he suddenly realized this and understood that this state of mind was extremely dangerous: his agitation, the irritation of his nerves would only increase. “Bad! Bad!… I'm going to say something stupid again.”

"Yes, yes! Don't worry! We have time, we have time," muttered Porfiry Petrovich, who, without any apparent intention, walked back and forth in the room, sometimes approaching the window, sometimes the desk, and then returning to the table; sometimes he avoided Raskolnikov's suspicious gaze; sometimes he stopped abruptly and looked his visitor straight in the face. It was an extraordinarily bizarre sight that this small, fat, round man was offering at that moment, whose evolutions recalled those of a ball bouncing from one wall to another.

"Nothing is urgent, nothing is urgent! But do you smoke? Do you have tobacco? Here, take a cigarette," he continued, offering a paquitos to the visitor. "You know, I receive you here, but my lodging is there, behind this partition. The State provides it for me. I am here only in a flying camp, because there were some arrangements to be made in my apartment. Now everything is ready, or nearly so. Do you know that it is a great thing to have a lodging provided by the State, eh, what do you think?"

“Yes, it's a great thing,” answered Raskolnikov, looking at him with a half-mocking air.

"A fine thing, a fine thing," repeated Porfiry Petrovich, who seemed to be thinking of something quite different. "Yes, a fine thing!" he cried suddenly, in a voice almost thundering, and stopping two paces from Raskolnikov, he suddenly fixed his eyes upon him. The incessant and foolish repetition of the phrase that a government apartment was a fine thing, contrasted with the serious, deep, enigmatical gaze he now fixed upon his visitor.

Raskolnikov's anger increased, and he could not help but address the examining magistrate with a mocking and rather imprudent challenge.

“You know,” he began, looking at him almost insolently and taking pleasure in this insolence, “it seems to be a legal rule, a principle for all examining magistrates, to begin the interview with trifles, or even with something serious, foreign to the question, in order to embolden the person they are interrogating, or rather in order to distract him, to lull his prudence; then suddenly, unexpectedly, they hit him in the middle of the forehead with the most dangerous question: isn't that right? It is a custom scrupulously observed in your profession?”

— So you think that if I told you about housing provided by the State, it was to…

As he said this, Porfiry Petrovich blinked his eyes, his face took on a mischievous expression for an instant, the little wrinkles on his forehead smoothed out, his little eyes became even narrower, the features of his face expanded, and, looking Raskolnikov straight in the eyes, he burst into a nervous, prolonged laugh that shook his whole body. The young man laughed himself, forcing himself a little; at this sight, Porfiry Petrovich's hilarity redoubled, to such an extent that the face of the investigating magistrate became almost crimson. Raskolnikov then felt a disgust that made him forget all prudence: he stopped laughing, frowned, and, all the time that Porfiry abandoned himself to this gaiety that seemed a little artificial, he fixed a hateful look on him. Neither one, however, had observed the other any more than the other. Porfiry had started laughing in the face of his visitor, who had taken the matter very badly, and he seemed to care very little about Raskolnikov's discontent. This last circumstance gave the young man a lot to think about: he thought he understood that his arrival had not in the least disturbed the investigating magistrate: it was, on the contrary, he, Raskolnikov, who had fallen into a trap; obviously there was some trap, some ambush that he did not know, the mine was perhaps already loaded and was going to explode in a moment...

Going straight to the point, he stood up and took his cap:

"Porphyry Petrovitch," he said in a resolute tone, but with a rather irritated air, "yesterday you expressed a desire to interrogate me." He laid particular stress on the word "interrogate."interrogation.) I have come to place myself at your disposal; if you have any questions to ask me, ask them; if not, permit me to withdraw. I cannot waste my time here; I have other things to do… I must go to the funeral of that official who was run over by a carriage and of whom… you have also heard, he added, and immediately regretted having added this phrase. Then he continued with increasing anger: All this bores me, do you understand? and it has been going on too long… It is, in part, what made me ill… In a word, he continued in an increasingly irritated voice, for he felt that the phrase about his illness was even more out of place than the other, in a word, please question me or allow me to leave at once… But if you question me, let it be in the form required by the procedure; otherwise, I do not permit it; until then, goodbye, since, for the moment, we have nothing to do with each other.

“Sir! But what are you saying? But what can I ask you?” the magistrate resumed, instantly ceasing to laugh. “Do not worry, I beg you.”

He invited Raskolnikov to sit down again, while he himself continued to pace the room.

"We have time, we have time, and all this is of no importance! On the contrary, I am so glad that you have come to our house... I am receiving you as a visitor. As for that cursed laughter, batuchka, Rodion Romanovitch, excuse me... I am a nervous man, you have greatly amused me by the finesse of your observation; there are times when, really, I begin to bounce like an elastic ball, and that for half an hour... I am a laughing man. My temperament even makes me fear apoplexy. But sit down, why are you standing?... I beg you, batuchka, otherwise I shall think you are angry..."

With his eyebrows still contracted, Raskolnikov was silent, listening and observing. However, he sat down.

"As for me, my dear Rodion Romanovitch, I will tell you something that will help you to understand my character," continued Porfiry Petrovitch, who was still pacing up and down the room, and as usual avoiding the eyes of his visitor.

"I live alone, you know, I don't go out in society and I'm unknown, add that I'm a man on the way back, already finished and ... and ... have you noticed, Rodion Romanovitch, that here, that is, in Russia, and especially in Petersburg circles, when two intelligent men come to meet who do not yet know each other well, but who esteem each other reciprocally, like you and me, for example, at this moment, they can not find anything to say to each other for a whole half-hour, they remain like petrified vis-à-vis each other? Everyone has a subject of conversation, the ladies, for example, the people of the world, the people of high society ... in all these circles one has something to talk about, it is mandatory; but people of the middle class, like us, are embarrassed and taciturn. Why is that, batuchka? Do we not have social interests? Or does it come from the fact that we are too honest people who do not want to deceive each other? I do not know. Well, what is your opinion? But get rid of your cap, it looks like you want to leave, and that makes me sad ... I am, on the contrary, so happy ...

Raskolnikov put down his cap. He remained silent, and, with knitted brows, listened to Porfiry's empty chatter. “He is only talking all this nonsense to distract my attention.”

"I don't offer you coffee, it's not the place, but can you spend five minutes with a friend, to give him a distraction?" continued the indefatigable Porfiry. You know, all these obligations of the service ... Do not be formal, batuchka, if you see me going and coming; excuse me, batuchka, I am afraid of hurting you, but movement is so necessary to me! I'm always sitting, and it's such a great happiness for me to be able to move for five minutes ... I have hemorrhoids ... I always intend to treat myself with gymnastics; the trapeze is said to be in great favor among the State Councilors, the current State Councilors, and even the Privy Councilors. Today, gymnastics has become a real science ... As for these duties of our charge, these interrogations and all this formalism ... it was you, batuchka, who spoke of it earlier ... well, you know, in fact, batuchka, Rodion Romanovitch, these interrogations sometimes confuse the magistrate more than the defendant ... You made this observation just now with as much wit as accuracy. (Raskolnikov had made no such observation.) We get confused, true, we lose the thread! As for our legal customs, I fully agree with you. What accused, even the most obtuse moujik, does not know that they will begin by asking him foreign questions to put him to sleep (according to your happy expression), since they will suddenly strike him with an axe in the sinciput, hee, hee, hee! in the sinciput (to use your ingenious metaphor), hee, hee! So you thought that when I spoke to you about housing I wanted to you ... hee, hee! You are a caustic man. Come on, I'm not going back to that! Ah! yes, by the way, one word leads to another, thoughts attract each other, - you were talking about the form in relation to the investigating magistrate ... But what is the form? You know, in many cases, form means nothing. Sometimes a simple conversation, a friendly conversation leads more surely to a result. The form will never disappear, let me reassure you on that point; but what, in the end, is the form, I ask you? One cannot force the examining magistrate to drag it around his feet all the time. The work of the investigator is, in its own way, a liberal art or something close to it, hee, hee!

Porfiry Petrovitch paused for a moment to take breath. He spoke without stopping, sometimes uttering pure nonsense, sometimes slipping in enigmatical phrases among his absurdities, and then again going off into nonsense. His walk about the room now resembled a run; he moved his big legs more and more quickly, and kept his eyes fixed on the floor; his right hand was thrust into his coat pocket, while with his left he continually made various gestures which had no connection with his words. Raskolnikov noticed, or fancied he noticed, that twice he stopped short in his rapid walk, as though he were listening attentively. "Is he expecting something?" he thought.

"You are perfectly right," Porphyry gaily replied, looking at the young man with a bonhomie that immediately put the latter on his guard, "our legal customs do indeed deserve your witty mockery, he! he! These procedures, supposedly inspired by a profound psychology, are very ridiculous and often even sterile ...

To return to the form, well! suppose that I am in charge of the investigation of a case, I know or rather I believe I know that the culprit is a certain gentleman ... Are you not preparing to follow the career of law, Rodion Romanovitch?

“Yes, I was studying…”

"Well, here is a little example that may be useful to you later," he said, "that is, do not think that I am trying to play the professor with you; God forbid that I should pretend to teach anything to a man who writes about crime in the newspapers! No, I am only taking the liberty of citing a small fact as an example," he continued, "supposing, then, that I had discovered the guilty party: why, I ask you, should I disturb him prematurely, even if I had evidence against him? Of course, another man, who did not have the same character, I would have him arrested immediately, but this one, why should I not let him walk around the city a bit, hee hee! No, I see that you do not understand very well; I will explain myself more clearly.

"If, for instance, I were to hurry too much to issue a warrant for his arrest, well, by that I should be giving him, so to speak, a moral support, he! he! you are laughing?" (Raskolnikov did not even think of laughing; he kept his lips tightly shut, and his burning eyes did not leave Porfiry Petrovich's face.) "Yet, in this case, it is so, for people are very different, though, unfortunately, the procedure is the same for all."

"But, since you have proof?" you will say to me.

— Oh, my God, batuchka, you know what evidence is: three-quarters of the time the evidence is for two purposes, and I, an investigating judge, am a man, therefore subject to error.

Now, I would like to give my investigation the absolute rigor of a mathematical demonstration; I would like my conclusions to be as clear, as indisputable as two times two make four! Therefore, if I have this gentleman arrested before the time is right, I will have no choice but to be convinced that it ishim, — I take away from myself the means of establishing his guilt later. And how is that? But because I give him a defined situation in some way; by putting him in prison, I calm him down, I make him return to his psychological state; henceforth he escapes me, he withdraws into himself: he finally understands that he is a prisoner.

If, on the contrary, I leave the presumed criminal perfectly alone, if I do not have him arrested, if I do not disturb him, but if at all hours, at all minutes, he is obsessed by the thought that I know everything, that I do not lose sight of him day or night, that he is the object of my tireless surveillance, — what will happen in these conditions? Infallibly he will be seized with vertigo, he will come to me himself, he will provide me with a quantity of weapons against him and will put me in a position to give the conclusions of my investigation a character of mathematical evidence, which is not lacking in charm.

If this method can succeed with an uncultivated moujik, it is not without efficacy when it is a question of an enlightened, intelligent, even distinguished man in some respects! For the important thing, my dear friend, is to guess in what direction a man is developed. This one is intelligent, I suppose, but he has nerves, nerves that are excited, sick! ... And the bile, the bile that you forget, what role it plays in all these people! I repeat to you, there is a real mine of information! And what do I care if he walks around the city freely? I can let him enjoy his rest, I know that he is my prey and that he will not escape me! In fact, where would he go? To a foreign country, you say? A Pole will escape to a foreign country, but not him, all the more so since I am watching him and my measures are taken accordingly. Will he retire to the interior of the country? But there live coarse moujiks, primitive Russians, devoid of civilization; this enlightened man would rather go to prison than live in such an environment, he! he!

Besides, all this means nothing yet, it is the accessory, the external side of the question. He will not run away, not only because he would not know where to go, but also, and above all, because,psychologically, he belongs to me, hee hee! How do you like that expression? By virtue of a natural law, he will not flee, even if he could. Have you seen the butterfly in front of the candle? Well, he will constantly turn around me like that insect around the flame; freedom will no longer be sweet to him; he will become more and more anxious, more and more bewildered; let me give him time, and he will commit such actions that his guilt will be clear as two and two make four! ... And always, always he will turn around me, describing circles that are increasingly tighter, until finally, bang! He will fly into my mouth and I will swallow him; it is very pleasant, hee hee! Don't you think?

Raskolnikov kept silent; pale and motionless, he continued to observe Porfiry's face with a painful effort of attention.

"This is a good lesson!" he thought, terrified. "It is no longer, as yesterday, the cat playing with the mouse. He does not speak to me thus for the mere pleasure of showing me his strength, he is far too intelligent for that... He must have some other aim, what is it? Go on, my friend, all you say is to frighten me! You have no proofs, and the man of yesterday does not exist! You simply want to throw me off my guard, you want to make me angry and strike the great blow, when you see me in that state; only you are mistaken, you will have your trouble for nothing! But why does he speak thus in veiled terms?... He is speculating on the irritation of my nervous system!... No, my friend, it will not work, whatever you have planned... We shall see a little what you have prepared there."

And he prepared himself to face bravely the terrible catastrophe he foresaw. From time to time he felt like throwing himself on Porphyry and strangling him on the spot. As soon as he entered the magistrate's office, his great fear was that he would not be able to control his anger. He felt his heart beating violently, his lips becoming dry and foam forming on them. However, he resolved to keep silent, realizing that, in his position, this was the best tactic: in this way, in fact, not only would he not compromise himself, but he might succeed in irritating his enemy and wringing some imprudent words from him. At least, that was Raskolnikov's hope.

“No, I see that you do not believe me, you think I am joking,” Porfiry went on; the examining magistrate was growing more and more cheerful, he kept up his little laugh, and he had resumed his walk up and down the room, — “no doubt you are right; God has given me a face that awakens only comic ideas in others; I am a buffoon; but excuse the language of an old man; you, Rodion Romanovitch, are in the flower of youth, and, like all young men, you appreciate human intelligence above all things. The piquancy of wit and the abstract deductions of reason seduce you.

To return to theparticular case"I will tell you, sir," he said, "that in the matter of which we were speaking just now, we must reckon with reality, with nature. It is a great thing, and sometimes it triumphs over the most consummate skill. Listen to an old man, I speak seriously, Rodion Romanovitch" (as he said this, Porfiry Petrovich, who was scarcely thirty-five, seemed to have grown all at once much older; a sudden change came over his whole person, and his voice too) "and, moreover, I am an honest man... Are you an honest man?" he asked, looking inquiringly into my face. "I think so," I answered, somewhat surprised. "Yes, I think so too," he continued, with a smile. "Well, then, let us be honest with one another; let us deal openly with each other. I want to be your friend, Rodion Romanovitch, for I feel drawn to you, and, moreover, I am interested in you. I want to help you, and, of course, I have my own object in doing so. You see, Rodion Romanovitch, I am a practical man. I love efficiency. I am a practical man, and I am interested in your case, because I believe you are a practical man too. I am convinced that you are not a man to throw yourself away for nothing. I am convinced that you are a man of great strength and resolution, and that you have a great future before you. I am interested in your future. I want to be your friend. I want to help you."

Well, I continue: wit is, in my opinion, a very fine thing, it is, so to speak, the ornament of nature, the consolation of life, and, with that, one can, it seems, easily fool a poor examining magistrate who himself is, moreover, often deceived by his own imagination, for he is a man! But nature comes to the aid of the poor examining magistrate, that's the trouble! And that's what the youth who trusts in his intelligence, the youth "who tramples on all obstacles" (as you said in such a fine and ingenious way), does not think about.

In theparticular caseIn the case we are dealing with, the guilty man, I admit, will lie superbly; but when he thinks he has only to reap the fruit of his skill, crack! he will faint in the very place where such an accident is most likely to be commented upon. Let us suppose that he can explain his syncope by a sickly condition, by the stifling atmosphere of the room; no matter, he has none the less given rise to suspicion! He has lied in an incomparable way, but he has not been able to take precautions against nature. That is where the trap lies!

Another time, carried away by his mocking humor, he will amuse himself by mystifying someone who suspects him and, for fun, he will pretend to be the criminal wanted by the police; but he will get too well into the skin of the good man, he will play his alleged comedywith too much naturalness., and that will be another clue. At the moment, his interlocutor may be fooled; but if the latter is not a fool, he will change his mind the next day. Our man will thus compromise himself at every moment! What am I saying? he will come of his own accord where he is not called, and he will spread imprudent words, allegories whose meaning will not escape anyone, he! he! He will come to ask why he has not yet been arrested, he! he! And that can happen to a character of a very fine mind, even to a psychologist and a man of letters! Nature is the most transparent mirror, it is enough to contemplate it! But why are you turning pale like that, Rodion Romanovitch! You may be too hot: do you want the window opened?

"Oh, don't worry about me, I beg you," cried Raskolnikov, and suddenly he began to laugh.

“Please, don't mind me!”

Porfiry stopped in front of him, waited a moment, and suddenly burst out laughing himself. Raskolnikov, whose hilarity had suddenly subsided, rose.

"Porfiry Petrovitch!" he said in a firm and loud voice, though he was still trembling all over, "I can no longer doubt that you suspect me of having murdered this old woman and her sister Elizabeth. I tell you on my side that I have long been sick of all this. If you have the right to arrest me, arrest me, and I will go with you. But I will not allow myself to be made a laughing-stock and to be tortured."

Suddenly his lips began to tremble, his eyes flashed fire, and his voice, hitherto restrained, reached its highest pitch.

"I won't allow it!" he cried suddenly, and he struck the table a violent blow with his fist.

"Do you understand that, Porfiry Petrovitch? I will not allow it!"

Ah! Lord! but what is the matter with you? cried the examining magistrate, apparently very much alarmed.

“Batushka! Rodion Romanovitch! My dear friend! But what is the matter with you?”

"I don't allow it!" repeated Raskolnikov.

"Batushka, a little lower! They'll hear you, they'll come, and then what shall we say? Think a little about that!" Porfiry Petrovitch whispered in a frightened voice, having brought his face close to that of his visitor.

"I don't allow it, I don't allow it!" Raskolnikov repeated mechanically, but this time in a low voice, so that Porfiry alone could hear him.

He ran to open the window.

"We must air the room! But if you drank a little water, my dear friend? You see, it's a little fit!"

He was already on his way to the door to give orders to a servant, when he noticed a carafe of water in a corner.

“Batuchka, drink,” he murmured, quickly approaching the young man with the carafe, “perhaps it will do you some good…”

Porphyry Petrovitch's terror and even solicitude seemed so little feigned that Raskolnikov was silent and began to examine him with a dull curiosity. He refused the water that was offered him.

“Rodion Romanovitch! My dear friend! But if you go on like this, you will go mad, I assure you! Drink, then, drink at least a few drops!”

He almost forced the glass of water into his hand. Raskolnikov mechanically raised it to his lips, but suddenly changed his mind and put it down with disgust on the table.

Yes, you had a little attack! You will do so much, my dear friend, that you will have a relapse of your illness, observed the investigating magistrate, who still seemed very disturbed, in the most affectionate tone.

"Lord! Is it possible to be so little careful of oneself? It's like Dmitri Prokofitch who came to see me yesterday. I admit that I have a caustic temper, that my character is awful, but, Lord! what significance has been given to harmless sallies! He came to see me yesterday, after your visit; we were at dinner, he talked, talked. I merely spread out my arms, but inwardly I said to myself: 'Ah! my God!' It was you who sent him, wasn't it? Sit down, then, batuchka; sit down, for Christ's sake!"

"No, it wasn't me! But I knew he had been to your place and why he had paid you that visit," Raskolnikov answered dryly.

— You knew that?

— Yes. Well, what do you conclude from that?

"I conclude, my dear Rodion Romanovitch, that I know a great deal more about you than you think I do. I know all about it! I know that you went out at dusk to the old woman's, and that you stayed there for two hours."to rent the apartmentYou pulled the bell, asked about the blood, and astonished the workmen and dvorniks. Oh, I understand the state of mind you were in then… But all the same, all these agitations will drive you mad! You are boiling with noble indignation, you have to complain first of fate, and then of the police. And so you go from place to place, as it were forcing people to formulate their accusations aloud. These stupid rumors are unbearable to you, and you want to have it all out at once. Is that true? Have I guessed right what feelings are guiding you?… But you are not content with upsetting yourself, you are upsetting my poor Razumikhin too, and it is really a pity to upset such a good fellow! His goodness makes him more than anyone else liable to catch the infection of your illness… When you are calmer, batuchka, I will tell you… But sit down, batuchka, for the love of Christ! I beg you, collect your thoughts, you are all upset; sit down.

Raskolnikov sat down, a feverish trembling shook his whole body. He listened with profound surprise to Porfiry Petrovich, who was lavishing demonstrations of interest on him. But he did not put any faith in the words of the examining magistrate, though he had a strange tendency to believe them. He had been extremely impressed when he heard Porfiry tell him about his visit to the old woman's lodgings: "How does he know that, and why does he tell me himself?" the young man thought.

"Yes, there has been a case in our judicial practice which was almost analogous, a morbid case," continued Porfiry. "A man accused himself of a murder he had not committed. And it is not enough to say that he declared himself guilty; he told a whole story, a hallucination of which he had been the victim, and his story was so plausible, seemed so much in accord with the facts, that it defied contradiction. How can this be explained? Without being in any way guilty, this man had been, in part, the cause of a murder. When he learned that he had, without knowing it, facilitated the work of the murderer, he was so grieved that his reason was affected, and he imagined himself to be the murderer himself! In the end, the Governing Senate examined the case, and it was discovered that the unfortunate man was innocent. Still, without the Governing Senate, it would have been all over with the poor devil! That is what is hanging over your head, my dear boy! One can also become a monomaniac when one goes at night to pull bell-ropes and ask questions about blood! You see, in the exercise of my profession, I have had the opportunity to study all this psychology. It is a similar attraction which sometimes drives a man to throw himself out of a window or from the top of a bell-tower... You are ill, Rodion Romanovitch! You were wrong to neglect your illness too much at the beginning. You should have consulted an experienced doctor, instead of having yourself treated by that fat Zosimov!... All this is the effect of delirium in you!..."

For a moment, Raskolnikov thought that all the objects around him were spinning. "Is it possible that he is still lying at this moment?" he wondered. And he tried to banish this idea, sensing to what excess of mad rage it could push him.

"I was not delirious, I was in full possession of my faculties!" he cried, while he racked his brain to try to fathom Porphyre's game. "I was in full possession of my faculties, do you hear?"

"Yes, I understand and I hear. You already said yesterday that you were not delirious, you even insisted on this point! I understand everything you can say! he! he! ...

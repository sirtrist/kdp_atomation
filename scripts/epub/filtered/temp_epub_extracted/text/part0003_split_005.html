It was a gentleman of a certain age, with a refined bearing and a reserved and severe countenance. He stopped first on the threshold, casting his eyes around him with a surprise that he did not try to conceal, and which was all the more disobliging. "Where on earth have I got to?" he seemed to be asking himself. It was with distrust and even with an affected fear that he contemplated the narrow and low room in which he found himself. His eyes retained the same expression of astonishment when they next fell upon Raskolnikov. The young man, in a very slovenly attire, was lying on his miserable divan. He did not move and began to examine the visitor in his turn. Then the latter, still preserving his haughty air, examined the unkempt beard and dishevelled hair of Razumihin, who, on his side, without moving from his place, stared at him with impertinent curiosity. For a minute there was an awkward silence for everybody. At last, the gentleman, probably understanding that his grand airs did not impress anybody, became a little more human and, politely though with a certain stiffness, addressed himself to Zosimov.

"Rodion Romanovitch Raskolnikoff, a gentleman who is a student or a former student?" he asked, stressing each syllable.

Zosimov slowly raised himself, and perhaps would have answered if Razumihin, to whom the question was not addressed, had not hastened to anticipate him:

“There he is, on the sofa! But what do you want?”

The unceremoniousness of these last words offended the gentleman with the important air; he made a movement towards Razumihin, but checked himself in time and turned sharply to Zosimov.

"There is Raskolnikov!" said the doctor carelessly, nodding towards the patient; then he yawned till his jaw cracked, pulled out an enormous gold watch from his pocket, looked at it, and put it back again.

As for Raskolnikov, still lying on his back, he did not say a word and did not stop staring at the newcomer, but all thought was absent from his gaze. Since he had torn himself away from contemplating the little flower, his face, extremely pale, betrayed an extraordinary suffering. One would have said that the young man had just undergone a painful operation or been subjected to the torture of the question. Gradually, however, the presence of the visitor aroused in him a growing interest: it was first surprise, then curiosity, and finally a kind of fear. When the doctor showed him by saying: "Here is Raskolnikov", our hero suddenly got up, sat down on the sofa, and, in a weak and broken voice, but with a defiant accent:

"Yes," he said, "I am Raskolnikov! What do you want?"

The gentleman looked at him attentively and replied in a dignified tone:

— Pierre Petrovitch Luzhin. I have reason to hope that my name is not entirely unknown to you.

But Raskolnikov, who had expected something quite different, merely looked at his interlocutor in silence and with a dazed air, as though the name of Pyotr Petrovitch had for the first time struck his ears.

"How is that?" asked Luzhin, a little disconcerted. "Can it be that you have not yet heard of me?"

In reply, Raskolnikov slowly sank back on the pillow, put his hands behind his head, and stared at the ceiling. Pyotr Petrovitch's face was visibly embarrassed. Zosimov and Razumihin were watching him with increasing curiosity, which completed his discomfiture.

"I presumed, I counted," he stammered, "that a letter posted ten days ago, perhaps even fifteen…

"Listen, why do you always stand by the door?" Razumihin suddenly interrupted. "If you have something to explain, then sit down; but you and Nastasia cannot both stand on the threshold; it is too narrow. Nastasya, stand aside, let him pass! Come in, here is a chair here! Squeeze in!"

He pushed his chair away from the table, left a small space between it and his knees, and waited, in a rather awkward position, for the visitor to "squeeze" through this passage. There was no way to refuse. Pyotr Petrovich squeezed himself, not without difficulty, to the chair and, after sitting down, looked at Razumikhin with a defiant air.

"Besides, don't mind us," he said in a loud voice; "Rodia has been ill for five days already, and he was delirious for three days, but now he is conscious, and has even eaten with appetite. Here is his doctor. I am a friend of Rodia's, a former student like himself, and at present I am acting as his nurse: so don't pay any attention to us, and go on with your conversation as if we were not here."

“I thank you. But will my presence and conversation not fatigue the patient?” asked Pierre Petrovitch, addressing Zosimoff.

“No, on the contrary, it will be a distraction for him,” replied the doctor in an indifferent tone, and he yawned again.

"Oh, he's been in his right mind for a long time now, since this morning!" added Razumihin, whose familiarity was so frank and good-natured that Pyotr Petrovitch began to feel more at ease. And besides, after all, this ill-dressed and unmannerly man was an excellent student.

"Hm!" Razumihin grunted loudly.

Loujine looked at him with a surprised air.

Loujine shrugged his shoulders.

— … Your mother had begun a letter to you even before my departure. Arriving here, I purposely delayed my visit for a few days to be sure that you would be informed of everything. But now, I see with astonishment…

I know, I know! Raskolnikov suddenly retorted, his face expressing violent irritation.

— You are the future? Well, I know that, and that's enough!

This language decidedly offended Piotr Petrovich, but he kept silent, wondering what it all meant. The conversation was momentarily interrupted. However, Raskolnikov, who, in order to answer him, had slightly turned to his side, suddenly began to examine him with marked attention, as if he had not had time to see him well before, or that something new had struck him in the person of the visitor. He therefore rose from the sofa to consider him more at ease. The fact is that Piotr Petrovich's whole appearance offered a certain something special that seemed to justify the appellation of "future" so cavalierly applied to this character.

In the first place, it was evident, and even too evident, that Pyotr Petrovitch had made haste to make use of his stay in the capital to "brush up" and put himself in good trim, in anticipation of the imminent arrival of his betrothed. This, of course, was only a very natural thing to do. Perhaps he was too conscious of the fact, and too anxious to show off his success; but still, it was pardonable for a man in his position. Pyotr Petrovitch was entirely dressed in new clothes, and his elegant attire gave rise to no criticism except that it was too new and too distinctly made for the purpose of impressing. With what respectful and ceremonious solicitude did he handle the elegant round hat which he had only just bought and had not yet had time to wear! With what care did he hold in his hand his charming new gloves, which he had not dared to put on, but only carried in his hand for show, with the air of a man who has just bought them. In his dress Pyotr Petrovitch was chiefly distinguished by the elegance of his light-coloured suit, a very coquettish light-coloured summer jacket, a pair of light-coloured trousers of the same shade, and a waistcoat of the same colour. His linen, too, was of the best quality, fresh and dainty, and his necktie was a dainty pink-striped cambric. Pyotr Petrovitch, it must be added, looked very distinguished in these new clothes and seemed to be much younger than his years.

His face, very fresh and not without distinction, was pleasantly framed by dark sideburns cut in chops, which brought out the bright whiteness of a carefully shaved chin. His hair was hardly gray, and his hairdresser had managed to curl it without giving him, as almost always happens, the ridiculous head of a German groom. If there was something unpleasant and antipathetic in this serious and rather beautiful physiognomy, it was due to other causes. After impolitely staring at Mr. Luzhin, Raskolnikov smiled mockingly, threw himself back on his pillow and began to contemplate the ceiling again.

But Mr. Luzhin seemed determined to take no offense at anything, and he pretended not to notice these strange ways. He even made an effort to resume the conversation:

“I am very sorry to find you in this state. If I had known you were ill, I would have come sooner. But, you know, I am so busy!... I also have a very important trial to follow in the Senate. I am not talking about the steps and concerns that you can guess yourself. I am expecting your family, that is, your mother and your sister, any moment now...

Raskolnikov seemed about to say something; his face expressed a certain agitation. Pyotr Petrovitch paused a moment, waited, but seeing that the young man remained silent, he went on:

— … At any moment. In anticipation of their imminent arrival, I looked for a place for them to live…

“Where?” asked Raskolnikov in a weak voice.

— Not far from here, Bakaléieff's house…

"It's in the Voznesensky Pereulok," Razumihin interrupted, "there are two floors rented out by the merchant Iouchine as lodgings; I've been there."

— Yes, there are furnished apartments to rent.

— It's a vilely dirty hovel, and, moreover, of ill repute; some nasty things have happened there; the devil knows who lives there!… I myself was brought there by a scandalous adventure. Besides, the lodgings are not expensive.

"Of course I could not know all that, seeing that I come from the provinces," replied Pyotr Petrovitch, with a touch of pique; "however, the two rooms I have taken are very clean, and as it is for a very short time... I have already decided on our future lodgings," he continued, addressing Raskolnikov.

— It is being arranged; for the moment, I myself live in a boarding house. I live two steps from here, at Madame Lippevechzel's, in the apartment of a young friend of mine, André Séménitch Lébéziatnikoff; it was he who told me about the Bakaléieff house.

"Lébéziatnikoff?" asked Raskolnikov slowly, as though the name had reminded him of something.

— Yes, André Séménitch Lébéziatnikoff, who works in a ministry. Do you know him?

"Yes... no..." replied Raskolnikov.

“Excuse me, your question had made me suppose that he was not unknown to you. I was his guardian once… He is a very nice young man… and who professes very advanced ideas. I like to hang out with young people: through them you learn what is new.”

Having finished these words, Pierre Petrovitch looked at his listeners with the hope of catching some sign of approval on their faces.

"From what point of view?" asked Razumihin.

From the most serious point of view, I mean from the point of view of social activity, replied Luzhin, delighted that he had been asked this question.

— You see, I hadn't been to Petersburg for ten years. All these new things, all these reforms, all these ideas have penetrated even to us provincials; but to see more clearly and to see everything, one must be in Petersburg. Now, according to me, it is by observing our young generations that one gets the best information. And, I admit, I was charmed…

— Of what?

— Your question is vast. I may be mistaken, but I believe I have noticed clearer views, a more critical spirit, a more reasoned activity…

“That's the truth,” Zosimoff said carelessly.

“Isn't that so?” Pyotr Petrovich repeated, rewarding the doctor with an amiable glance. “You will agree yourself,” he continued, turning to Razumikhin, “that there is progress at least in the scientific and economic order…”

— A commonplace!

“No, it is not a commonplace! For instance, if I am told: ‘Love your neighbor,' and I follow this advice, what will happen?” said Luzhin, with perhaps too much haste; “I will tear my coat in two, give half to my neighbor, and we will both be half naked. As the Russian proverb says: ‘If you chase two hares, you will catch neither one.' Science, on the other hand, tells me to love only myself, since everything in the world is based on self-interest. If you love only yourself, you will manage your affairs properly, and your coat will remain whole. Political economy adds that the more private fortunes there are in a society, in other words, the more whole coats there are, the more solidly based and happily organized that society is. Therefore, by working only for myself, I also work, in fact, for everyone else, and my neighbor gets a little more than half a coat, and this is not through private and individual generosity, but as a result of general progress. The idea is simple; unfortunately, it has taken a long time to make its way, to triumph over chimera and dream; yet it does not seem to take much intelligence to understand…”

"Pardon, I belong to the category of fools," interrupted Razumihin. "So let's drop the subject. I had a purpose in beginning this conversation; but for the last three years I have been so pestered with all this talk, all these banalities, that I blush to speak of them and even to hear them spoken of before me. Naturally, you have hastened to display your theories before us; it's very excusable, and I don't blame you for it. I wanted to know what you are, because, you see, all these people have rushed into the public arena of late, and, never seeking anything but their own personal interests, have muddled everything up with their theories and discussions. Enough of it!"

"Sir," said Luzhin, stung to the quick, "is that a way of telling me that I too…"

"Oh, never in my life! How could you think so? Come, that's enough!" said Razumihin, and without paying any more attention to him, he resumed the conversation with Zosimov which had been interrupted by the arrival of Pyotr Petrovitch.

The latter had the good sense to accept the student's explanation as it was. Besides, he was determined to leave in two minutes.

"Now that we have made acquaintance," he said to Raskolnikov, "I hope our relations will continue after your return to health and will become closer, thanks to the circumstances you know of... I wish you a speedy recovery."

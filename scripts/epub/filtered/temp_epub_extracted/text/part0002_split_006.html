Raskolnikov learned later why the merchant and his wife had invited Elizabeth to their house. The matter was very simple. A foreign family in straitened circumstances wanted to sell some of their belongings, which consisted mainly of women's clothing and linen. These people were therefore looking to get in touch with a woman who bought and sold used clothing; and Elizabeth was a woman who bought and sold used clothing. She had a large clientele because she was very honest and always gave the final price: with her, there was no haggling. In general, she spoke little; as we have already said, she was very gentle and very timid.

But for some time past Raskolnikoff had become superstitious, and afterwards, when he reflected upon the whole affair, he always inclined to the belief that there were strange and mysterious causes at work in it. Last winter, a student of his acquaintance, Pokoreff, who was going to Kharkoff, had given him, in conversation, the address of the old woman Alena Ivanovna, in case he should need to borrow money. He did not go to her for a long time, because the proceeds of his lessons allowed him to live. Six weeks before the events we are describing, he remembered the address; he had two things on which he could raise money: an old silver watch which had belonged to his father, and a small gold ring, ornamented with three small red stones, which his sister had given him as a keepsake when they parted.

Raskolnikov decided to take the ring to Alena Ivanovna. At first sight, and before he knew anything particular about her, this old woman inspired him with a violent aversion. After receiving from her two "little bills," he went into a poor traktir that he met on his way. There he ordered tea, sat down, and began to think. A strange idea, still in an embryonic state in his mind, occupied him exclusively.

At a table next to his own, an undergraduate whom he did not remember ever having seen before was sitting with an officer. The two young men had just played billiards, and were now drinking tea. Suddenly Raskolnikov heard the undergraduate give the officer the address of Alena Ivanovna, the widow of a college secretary and a pawnbroker. This alone seemed somewhat strange to our hero: they were talking about a person whom he had just visited a short time ago. Of course, it was a pure coincidence, but at that moment he was struggling against an impression that he could not overcome, and here, as if on cue, someone came to reinforce this impression in him; the undergraduate was indeed giving his friend various details about Alena Ivanovna.

"It's a famous resource," he said, "there's always a way to get money from her. Rich as a Jew, she can lend five thousand rubles at a time, and yet she accepts objects worth a ruble as collateral. She's a godsend for many of our people. But what a horrible shrew!"

And he began to tell how she was spiteful and capricious, that she would not even give twenty-four hours' grace, and that a pledge not taken out on the appointed day was irrevocably lost to the debtor; that she lent on an object the quarter of its value and took five and even six per cent. interest per month, etc. The student, in a talkative mood, added that this dreadful old woman was very small, which did not prevent her from beating every moment and keeping in complete dependence her sister Elizabeth, who was at least two arshins eight vershoks in height.

"That's another phenomenon!" he exclaimed, and laughed.

The conversation then turned to Elisabeth. The student spoke of her with marked pleasure and always with a laugh. The officer listened to his friend with great interest, and asked him to send this Elisabeth to mend his linen. Raskolnikov did not lose a word of this conversation; he learned a lot of things. Younger than Alena Ivanovna, of whom she was only the blood sister, Elisabeth was thirty-five years old. She worked day and night for the old woman. In addition to combining in the house the job of cook and that of laundress, she did sewing work that she sold, went to wash floors outside, and gave all she earned to her sister. She did not dare accept any order, any work without first obtaining Alena Ivanovna's permission. The latter, Elisabeth knew, had already made her will, under which her sister inherited only her furniture. Desiring to have perpetual prayers for the repose of her soul, the old woman had bequeathed all her fortune to a monastery in the government of N... Elisabeth belonged to the bourgeois class, and not to the chin. She was a girl of disproportionate height and lankiness, with long feet always shod in worn-out shoes, otherwise very clean on her person. What surprised and amused the student most was that Elisabeth was constantly pregnant...

On his return from the Haymarket he threw himself on his sofa, where he remained sitting motionless for an hour. The room was dark; he had no candle, and, moreover, it would not have occurred to him to light one. He could never remember whether during that time he had thought of anything. At last the feverish shivering of the previous night seized him again, and he thought with satisfaction that he could just as well lie down on the sofa. A leaden sleep soon fell upon him.

He slept much longer than usual and without dreaming. Nastasia, who came into his room the next morning at ten o'clock, had great difficulty in waking him. The servant brought him bread and, as on the previous day, the remains of her own tea.

"He's not up yet!" she exclaimed indignantly. "Can anyone sleep like that!"

Raskolnikov rose with difficulty. His head ached. He stood up, walked about the room, and then fell back on the sofa again.

"Again!" cried Nastasia, "but you are ill, then?"

He did not reply.

— Would you like some tea?

"Later," he said with difficulty; then he closed his eyes and turned to the wall. Nastasia stood over him for a while, looking at him.

"He may be ill," she said, as she went away.

At two o'clock she came back with soup. She found Raskolnikov still lying on the sofa. He had not touched the tea. The servant was angry and began to shake the lodger violently.

"What are you doing sleeping like that?" she said, looking at him with a contemptuous air.

He sat down, but did not answer a word, and kept his eyes fixed on the ground.

“Are you ill or not?” asked Nastasia.

This second question met with no more response than the first.

“You should go out,” she said after a silence; “the open air would do you good. You are going to eat, aren't you?”

“Later,” he said in a weak voice; “go away!” And he dismissed her with a gesture.

She stayed a moment longer, looked at him with an expression of pity, and finally went out.

After a few minutes he raised his eyes, looked long at the tea and soup, and began to eat.

He swallowed three or four spoonfuls without appetite, almost mechanically. His headache had calmed down a little. When he had finished his light meal, he lay down again on the sofa, but he could not fall asleep and remained motionless, lying on his stomach, his face buried in the pillow. His reverie constantly evoked bizarre scenes; most often, he imagined himself to be in Africa; he was part of a caravan stopped in an oasis; palm trees grew around the camp, the camels were resting from their fatigue, the travelers were having dinner; he himself was quenching his thirst in the current of a clear fountain: the bluish and deliciously cool water allowed to see at the bottom of the stream pebbles of various colors and sands with golden reflections.

Suddenly, the sound of a clock reached his ears distinctly. This noise made him start. Returning to the sense of reality, he raised his head, looked towards the window, and, after calculating the time it could be, got up hurriedly. Then, walking on tiptoe, he approached the door, opened it very softly and began to listen on the square. His heart was beating violently. But the stairs were perfectly silent. It seemed that everyone was sleeping in the house ... "How could I have let myself be cornered at the last moment? Since yesterday, how have I not done anything, prepared anything?" he wondered, not understanding such negligence ... And yet, it was perhaps six o'clock that had just struck.

To his inertia and torpor there suddenly succeeded an extraordinary feverish activity. Besides, the preparations did not require much time. He tried to think of everything, to forget nothing; but his heart continued to beat with such force that his breathing became difficult. First, he had to make a noose and adapt it to his overcoat; it was a matter of a minute. He searched in the linen that he had stuffed under the pillow for an old dirty shirt, which was, moreover, too worn to be worn anymore. Then, with the help of rags torn from this shirt, he made a halter one vershok wide and eight long.

After folding it in half, he took off his summer coat, which was made of thick, sturdy cotton cloth (it was the only outer garment he owned), and he began to sew the two ends of the chain together inside, under his left armpit. His hands were trembling as he did this work; he nevertheless accomplished it with such success that, when he put his coat back on, no trace of the stitching appeared on the outside. He had procured the needle and thread long ago, and he had only to take them from the drawer of his little table.

As for the noose, intended to hold the axe, it was a very ingenious trick, which had come to him fifteen days earlier. To show himself in the street with an axe in his hand was impossible. On the other hand, to hide the weapon under his overcoat was to condemn himself to keep his hand on it constantly, and this attitude would have attracted attention, whereas, given the noose, he had only to insert the iron of the axe, and it remained suspended under his armpit the whole time of the journey, without danger of falling. He could even prevent it from swinging: for that he had only to hold the end of the handle with his hand in the side pocket of his overcoat. Given the size of this garment, - a real sack, - the movement of the hand inside could not be noticed from the outside.

This task completed, Raskolnikov stretched his arm under his "Turkish" divan and, inserting his fingers into a crack in the floor, withdrew from this hiding place thewagerof which he had taken care to provide himself in advance. In truth, this pledge was not a pledge: it was simply a small polished wooden block, about the length and thickness of an silver cigarette case. During one of his walks, the young man had accidentally found this piece of wood in a courtyard belonging to a carpentry shop. He joined it to a small iron plate, thin and polished, but of smaller dimensions, which he had also picked up in the street. After crossing the block and the iron plate against each other, he tied them firmly together with a thread, then wrapped the whole thing in a piece of white paper.

This little package, to which he had tried to give as elegant an appearance as possible, was then tied in such a way that the knot was quite difficult to untie. It was a way of momentarily occupying the old woman's attention: while she was struggling with the knot, the visitor could seize the opportune moment. The iron plate had been added to give more weight to the supposed pledge, so that, at least in the first moment, the usurer would not suspect that she was being brought a simple piece of wood. Raskolnikoff had just put the object in his pocket when he suddenly heard someone shouting from outside:

"Six o'clock has been struck long ago!"

— For a long time! my God!

He rushed to the door, listened, and began to descend his thirty steps, making no more noise than a cat. The most important thing remained: to go and get the axe that was in the kitchen. He had decided long ago that he had to use an axe. He had a kind of pruning shears at home, but this instrument did not inspire him with any confidence, and above all he did not trust his strength; so it was on the axe that his choice finally settled. Let us note, in this regard, a singular peculiarity: as his resolutions took on a determined character, he felt more and more their absurdity and horror. Despite the terrible struggle that was going on inside him, he could never admit for a single instant that he would come to carry out his plans.

Moreover, if all the questions had been settled, all doubts removed, all difficulties smoothed out, he would probably have renounced his plan on the spot as an absurd, monstrous, and impossible thing. But there still remained a host of points to be cleared up, problems to be solved. As for getting the axe, this trifle did not worry Raskolnikov at all, for nothing could be easier. The fact was that Nastasia, especially in the evening, was almost never at home. She was constantly running out to visit friends or shopkeepers, and the quarrels her mistress had with her were always on this account.

When the time came, it would be enough to go quietly into the kitchen and take the axe, and then return it to the same place an hour later (when everything was over). But perhaps it would not go so smoothly: "Suppose," the young man said to himself, "that in an hour, when I come to return the axe, Nastasia is back. Naturally, in that case, I will have to wait for the servant to leave the kitchen again before I can go in. But if, during that time, she notices the axe is missing, she will start looking for it, she will grumble, who knows? she might even cause a stir in the house, and that will be a circumstance that will be used against me, or at least that can be used against me!"

However, these were still only details, which he did not want to think about; besides, he did not have time for them. He was thinking about the main thing, determined to deal with the accessory only when he had made up his own mind on the main issue. This last condition, the most essential of all, seemed to him decidedly unrealizable. Thus, he could not imagine that at some point he would stop thinking, get up and - go there directly ... Even in his recentrepetition(that is, in the visit he had paid to the old woman to sound the ground definitively), he had nearly notrepeatedseriously. An actor without conviction, he had not been able to sustain his role and had fled, indignant with himself. Yet, from a moral point of view, Raskolnikov had reason to consider the question as resolved. His casuistry, sharp as a razor, had cut through all objections, but, no longer finding any in his mind, he tried to find them outside. It was as if, carried away by an blind, irresistible, superhuman power, he was desperately seeking a fixed point to which he could cling. The incidents so unforeseen the day before acted on him in an almost absolutely automatic way. Like a man who has let the edge of his coat catch in a cogwheel is soon seized himself by the machine.

The first question that occupied him, and to which, moreover, he had thought many times, was this: Why are almost all crimes so easily discovered, and why are the traces of almost all criminals so easily found?

He gradually arrived at various curious conclusions. According to him, the main reason for the fact was less in the material impossibility of hiding the crime than in the personality of the criminal himself: almost always the latter found himself experiencing, at the moment of the crime, a decrease in will and understanding; that is why he behaved with childish foolishness, phenomenal lightness, even when circumspection and prudence were most necessary to him.

Raskolnikoff likened this eclipse of judgment and this failure of the will to a morbid affection which developed by degrees, reached its maximum of intensity shortly before the commission of the crime, subsisted in the same form at the time of the crime and for some time afterwards (more or less long according to individuals), to cease afterwards, as all diseases cease. One point to be cleared up was whether the disease determines the crime or whether the crime itself, by virtue of its own nature, is not always accompanied by some morbid phenomenon; but the young man did not yet feel capable of resolving this question.

Reasoning in this way, he persuaded himself that he, personally, was safe from such moral upheavals, that he would retain the fullness of his intelligence and will throughout the duration of his enterprise, for the sole reason that his enterprise "was not a crime" ... We will not report the series of arguments that had led him to this last conclusion. Let us say that, in his concerns, the practical side, the purely material difficulties of execution remained completely in the background. "If I only keep my presence of mind, my strength of will, and when the time to act comes, I will overcome all obstacles ..." But he did not get to work. He believed less than ever in the final persistence of his resolutions, and when the hour came, he woke up as if from a dream.

He had not yet reached the bottom of the stairs when a very insignificant circumstance upset his plans. On reaching the landing where his landlady lived, he found the kitchen door wide open, as usual, and glanced cautiously into the room: in Nastasia's absence, was not the landlady herself there, and if she was not there, had she shut the door of her room? Could she not see him from her room when he went in to get the axe? That was what he wanted to make sure of. But what was his amazement when he saw that Nastasia was in the kitchen! She was busy, moreover: she was taking linen out of a basket and hanging it on lines. At the sight of the young man, the servant girl, interrupting her work, turned to him and looked at him until he had gone away.

He turned his eyes away and passed by without appearing to notice anything. But that was the end of it; he had no axe! This disappointment dealt him a terrible blow.

"And where did I get it from," he thought, as he descended the last steps of the staircase, "where did I get it from that just at this moment Nastasia would infallibly come out? How did I get that into my head?"

He was crushed, as if annihilated. In his anger, he felt a need to mock himself. A wild anger boiled in his being.

He stood irresolute under the gateway. He had no desire to go out into the street, to go out for no purpose, to show off, but it was still more unpleasant to go back home. "To think that I have lost such a fine opportunity forever!" he muttered, standing in front of the dark porter's lodge, which was also open.

Suddenly he started. In the lodge, two paces from Raskolnikov, something gleamed under a bench on the left. The young man looked around him, but there was no one. He went up to the lodge very quietly, descended two steps, and called the dvornik in a low voice. "Well, he's not at home! Besides, he must have gone out, since he left his door open." Quick as lightning, he darted to the axe (it was an axe) and pulled it out from under the bench, where it lay between two logs. Then he put the weapon in his knotted handkerchief, thrust his hands into his pockets, and went out of the lodge. No one noticed him! "It was not my intelligence that helped me here, but the devil!" he thought with a strange smile. The lucky chance that had just befallen him contributed powerfully to his encouragement.

Once in the street, he walked calmly,seriously, without hurrying, for fear of arousing suspicion. He hardly looked at the passers-by, he even tried not to look at anyone and to attract as little attention as possible. Suddenly he thought of his hat. "My God! the day before yesterday I had money, I could have bought a cap!" An imprecation burst from the depths of his soul.

A glance thrown by chance into a shop where there was a clock against the wall told him that it was already seven-ten. Time was pressing, and yet he had to make a detour, for he did not want to be seen arriving from that side of the house.

Formerly, when he tried to picture to himself the situation he was now in, he sometimes imagined that he would be very frightened. Now, contrary to his expectation, he was not at all frightened. Thoughts foreign to his enterprise occupied his mind, but they were never for long. As he passed the Yusupov Garden, he thought that it would be a good thing to have monumental fountains on all the public squares to cool the air. Then, by a series of imperceptible transitions, he came to think that if the Summer Garden took up the whole expanse of the Champ de Mars and even joined up with the garden of the Mikhailovsky Palace, Petersburg would gain a great deal of profit and pleasure ...

"It is thus, no doubt, that people led to execution fix their thoughts on all the objects they meet on the way." This idea came into his mind, but he hastened to banish it... However, he approaches: here is the house, here is the main door. Suddenly he hears a clock strike a single blow. "How! is it seven-thirty? It is impossible; it is certainly fast!"

This time, too, chance served Raskolnikov's purpose. As if by design, just as he arrived in front of the house, an enormous hay cart was entering the gate, almost filling its entire width. The young man was thus able to cross the threshold without being seen, by slipping through the narrow passage left free between the cart and the wall.

When he was in the courtyard, he turned sharply to the right. On the other side of the cart, people were arguing; he could hear them shouting. But no one noticed him, and he met no one. Several of the windows that looked out on this immense square courtyard were open at the time; however, he did not raise his head, — he did not have the strength. His first move was to reach the old woman's staircase, which was on the right.

Catching his breath and holding his hand on his heart to suppress its beating, he set about climbing the stairs, not without first making sure that his axe was firmly attached by the running knot. He listened at every moment. But the staircase was completely deserted, all the doors were closed; he did not meet a soul. On the second floor, it is true, an unoccupied apartment was open, and painters were working there. These, however, did not see Raskolnikov. He stopped for a moment, thought, and continued his ascent. "No doubt it would be better if they were not there, but ... there are still two floors above them."

Here is the fourth floor, here is the door of Alena Ivanovna; the apartment opposite is unoccupied. On the third floor, the apartment directly below that of the old woman is also empty, apparently: the visiting card that was nailed to the door is no longer there, the tenants have left! ... Raskolnikov was choking. He hesitated for a second: "Wouldn't I do better to leave?" But, without answering this question, he listened: no noise came from the usurer's house. In the staircase, the same silence. After listening for a long time, the young man cast a last glance around him and felt his axe again. "Am I not too pale?" he thought, "do I not look too agitated? She is suspicious... If I waited a little longer... to let my emotion calm down?"

But, far from abating, the pulsations of his heart became more and more violent... He could not hold out any longer, and, slowly advancing his hand towards the bell-cord, he pulled it towards him. After half a minute, he rang again, this time a little louder.

There was no answer. To ring the bell like a deaf man would have been useless, even awkward. The old woman was certainly at home; but, naturally suspicious, she must be all the more so at this moment, when she was alone. Raskolnikov knew some of Alena Ivanovna's habits. And again he applied his ear to the door. Had the circumstances developed in him a special acuteness of sensation (which is generally difficult to admit), or was the noise really easily perceptible?

At any rate, his hearing suddenly distinguished a hand cautiously placing itself on the lock and a dress brushing against the door. Someone inside was playing exactly the same game as he was on the landing. Someone, standing near the lock, was listening, trying to conceal his presence, and probably also had his ear glued to the door.

Not wishing to appear to be hiding, the young man made a point of moving around a bit noisily and grumbling rather loudly; then he rang for the third time, but softly, calmly, without his ring betraying the slightest impatience. This minute left Raskolnikov with an indelible memory.

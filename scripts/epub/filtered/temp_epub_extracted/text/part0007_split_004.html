Raskolnikov followed him.

“What does this mean?” exclaimed Svidrigaïlov, turning round. “I thought I had told you…”

— What?

They both stopped and for an instant measured each other with their eyes.

"In your half-drunkenness," replied Raskolnikov, "you told me enough to convince me that, far from having abandoned your odious plans against my sister, you are more occupied with them than ever. I know that this morning my sister received a letter. You have not wasted your time since your arrival in Petersburg. That in the course of your comings and goings you have found a woman, is possible, but that means nothing. I desire to make sure personally..."

Of what? Raskolnikov himself could scarcely have said.

“Really! Do you want me to call the police?”

— Call her!

They stopped again facing each other. At last Svidrigailov's face changed. Seeing that his threat did not in the least intimidate Raskolnikov, he suddenly resumed his most cheerful and friendly tone:

"What a funny fellow you are! I purposely did not speak to you about your affair, in spite of the very natural curiosity it has aroused in me. I wanted to put it off till another time; but, in truth, you would try the patience of a dead man. Come, come with me; only, I warn you, I only go home to get some money; then I shall go out, take a carriage, and go and spend the whole evening in the Islands. “I have business in your house; but I am not going to your house, I am going to Sophie Semyonovna's: I must apologize to her for not having been at her mother-in-law's funeral.”

"As you please; but Sophie Semenovna is not at home. She has gone to take the three children to a lady of my acquaintance, who is the head of several orphanages. I have given this lady the greatest pleasure by handing her some money for Catherine Ivanovna's babies, and a pecuniary donation for her institutions; and finally, I have told her the story of Sophie Semenovna, without omitting any details. “You find it delicate to listen at doors?”

"Ha! ha!" replied Svidrigailov, laughing. "I should have been surprised if you had not made that observation! "I am not thinking of that at all," Raskolnikov replied with disgust.

"I understand: you are asking yourself if you have acted according to morality, as befits a man and a citizen. You should have asked yourself the question earlier, now it is a little untimely, eh! eh! If you think you have committed a crime, blow your brains out; isn't that what you want to do?

Svidrigaïloff tried to smile a friendly smile, but his face refused to obey him. His heart was beating violently, and his chest was oppressed. Svidrigaïloff occupied two rather large furnished rooms. Dounia looked around her with suspicion, but she found nothing suspicious either in the furniture or in the arrangement of the rooms. “Look at this large room, the second one. Notice that door, which is locked. Next to it is a chair, the only one in the two rooms.

"Pierre Petrovitch!" she cried, "protect me! Make this stupid creature understand that she has no right to speak so to a noble and unfortunate lady, that it is not allowed... I shall complain to the governor-general himself... She will have to answer... In memory of the hospitality you received at my father's, come to the aid of orphans."

“Pardon, madame… Pardon, pardon, madame,” said Pyotr Petrovitch, making a gesture to push the petitioner aside, “I have never had the honor, as you know yourself, of knowing your papa… pardon, madame (someone laughed loudly)! and I have no intention of taking sides in your continual quarrels with Amalia Ivanovna… I came here for a matter which concerns me personally… I desire to have an immediate explanation with your stepdaughter, Sofya… Ivanovna… That is her name, I believe? Allow me to enter…”

And leaving Katerina Ivanovna there, Pyotr Petrovich went to the corner of the room where Sonia was sitting.

Catherine Ivanovna was rooted to the spot. She could not understand how Pyotr Petrovich could deny having been her father's guest. This hospitality, which existed only in her imagination, had become an article of faith with her. What also struck her was Luzhin's dry, haughty, and even threatening tone. At his appearance, silence gradually returned. The lawyer's proper attire was too much at odds with the slovenliness of the lodgers of Madame Lippevechsel; everyone felt that only an exceptional motive could explain the presence of this person in such a milieu; so everyone was expecting an event. Raskolnikov, who was standing next to Sonia, moved aside to let Pyotr Petrovich pass; the latter did not seem to notice the young man. A moment later, Lebeziatnikov appeared in turn; but instead of entering the room, he remained on the threshold, listening with curiosity without being able to understand what it was all about.

“Pardon me for disturbing your gathering, but I am forced to do so by a rather important matter,” began Pyotr Petrovich, without addressing anyone in particular; “I am even glad to be able to explain myself before a large company. Amalia Ivanovna, in your capacity as the owner of the house, I beg you very humbly to lend your ear to the conversation I am going to have with Sofya Ivanovna.” Then, addressing the young girl, who was extremely surprised and already frightened, he added:

“Sophie Ivanovna, immediately after your visit, I noticed the disappearance of a banknote worth one hundred roubles, which was lying on a table in the room of my friend Andrei Semenovich Lebeziatnikoff. If you know what has become of this note and if you tell me, I give you, in the presence of all these people, my word of honor that the matter will not have any consequences. In the opposite case, I will be forced to resort to very serious measures, and then… you will have to blame only yourself.”

A profound silence followed these words. Even the children stopped crying. Sonia, pale as death, looked at Luzhin without being able to answer. She seemed not to have understood yet. A few seconds passed.

"Well, what do you say?" asked Pierre Petrovich, attentively observing the young girl.

"I don't know... I don't know anything..." she finally said in a weak voice.

"No?" asked Luzhin, and he let a few more seconds pass; then he continued in a severe tone:

“Think about it, mademoiselle, make your reflections, I will give you time. You see, if I were less sure of my facts, I would be careful not to make such a formal accusation against you: I have too much experience in business to expose myself to a defamation suit. This morning, I went to negotiate several securities representing a nominal value of three thousand rubles. On returning home, I counted the money, Andrei Semenovich was a witness.

"After counting out two thousand three hundred roubles, I put them in a wallet and put it in the side pocket of my frock coat. On the table there remained about five hundred roubles in banknotes; there were, in particular, three hundred-rouble notes. It was then that, at my invitation, you came to my house, and during the whole time of your visit you were in a state of extraordinary agitation. Three times even you got up to go, although our conversation was not yet over. Andrei Semenovich can attest to all this."

You will not deny, I think, mademoiselle, that I sent for you by Andrei Semenovitch for the sole purpose of talking to you about the unfortunate situation of your relative Catherine Ivanovna (at whose house I could not dine) and of the means of helping her by subscription, lottery, or otherwise. You thanked me with tears in your eyes (I go into all these details to prove that not a single circumstance has escaped my memory). Then I took a ten-ruble note from the table and gave it to you as a first aid to your relative. Andrei Semenovitch saw all this. Then I saw you to the door, and you left, showing the same signs of agitation as before.

After you left, I talked for about ten minutes with Andrei Semenovich. Finally he left me; I went up to the table to put away the rest of my money, and to my great surprise I found that a hundred-ruble note was missing. Now judge for yourself: I cannot suspect Andrei Semenovich! I cannot even conceive of such an idea. I could not have made a mistake in my accounts, either, because I had just checked them a minute before you came. You will agree yourself: recalling your agitation, your haste to leave, and the fact that you had your hands on the table for a certain time, and finally considering your social position and the habits it implies, I had to, in spite of myself, in spite of my own will, stop at a cruel, but legitimate suspicion!

Convinced as I am of your guilt, I repeat that I know what I am exposing myself to by bringing this accusation against you. However, I do not hesitate to do so, and I will tell you why: it is solely, mademoiselle, because of your black ingratitude! How? I call you to me because I am interested in your unfortunate relative; I make you a gift of ten roubles, and that is how you reward me! No, that is not good! You need a lesson. Reflect, go back into yourself: I urge you as your best friend, for that is the best thing you can do at the moment! Otherwise, I will be inflexible! Well, confess?

"I haven't taken anything from you," Sonia whispered in terror. "You gave me ten roubles, here they are, take them back."

The young girl took her handkerchief out of her pocket, untied a knot she had made in it, and took out a ten-ruble note, which she handed to Luzhin.

“So you persist in denying the theft of the hundred roubles?” he said reproachfully, without taking the note.

Sonia looked around her and saw nothing but severe, irritated, or mocking expressions on all the faces. She glanced at Raskolnikov. The young man was standing with his arms crossed against the wall, and he was gazing at her with burning eyes.

"Oh, Lord!" she moaned.

“Amalia Ivanovna, we shall have to inform the police; and I therefore request you very humbly to send for the dvornik,” said Luzhin in a soft and even caressing voice.

—God of mercy!"I knew she was a thief!" cried Madame Lippevechzel, clapping her hands together.

"You knew it?" repeated Pyotr Petrovitch. "That means that certain facts had already led you to suspect this. I beg you, most honorable Amalia Ivanovna, to recall the words you have just uttered. There are witnesses present."

There was a great deal of noisy conversation on all sides. The audience was becoming agitated.

"How !" cried Katerina Ivanovna, suddenly roused from her stupor, and with a rapid movement she flew at Luzhin; "how ! you accuse her of theft Her, Sonia Oh, you are a scoundrel, a scoundrel !" Then she rushed impulsively to the girl and embraced her warmly with her emaciated arms.

Sonia! How could you accept ten roubles from him! Oh! you fool! Give them here! Give me the money at once.

— Look!

Catherine Ivanovna took the note from Sonia, crumpled it in her fingers, and threw it in Luzhin's face. The crumpled paper hit Pyotr Petrovich and then bounced on the floor. Amalia Ivanovna hurried to pick it up. The businessman was angry.

“Hold that madwoman!” he cried.

At this moment several persons came to stand on the threshold beside Lebeziatnikoff; among them were the two ladies from the provinces.

"Mad, you say? You call me mad, you imbecile?" cried Catherine Ivanovna.

"Have you seen this idiot?" she added, going from one lodger to another, and pointing to Luzhin; suddenly she caught sight of Amalia Ivanovna, and her wrath knew no bounds.

"What! you too, sausage-seller, you too, infamous Prussian, you say she is a thief! Ah! if it is possible! But she has not left the room: on leaving your house, you rascal, she came immediately to sit down at table with us, all saw her! She sat down next to Rodion Romanovitch!... Search her! At the same time, Katerina Ivanovna seized Luzhin by the arm and dragged him towards Sonia.

"Let her be searched by anyone you like!" cried Katerina Ivanovna; "Sonia, show them your pockets! There! there! Look, monster, you see she's empty; there was a handkerchief, but nothing else, as you can see for yourself! Now the other pocket! there! there! you see!"

Not content with emptying Sonia's pockets, Katerina Ivanovna turned them inside out, one after the other. But at the moment when she thus exposed the lining of the right pocket, a small piece of paper escaped from it, described a parabola in the air, and fell at Luzhin's feet. All saw it, and several cried out. Pyotr Petrovitch bent down to the floor, picked up the paper between his two fingers, and unfolded it.in publicIt was a hundred-rouble note, folded in eight. Pyotr Petrovich held it up for everyone to see, so that there could be no doubt about Sonia's guilt.

“Thief! Out of here! The police, the police!” screamed Madame Lippevechzel. “She must be sent to Siberia! Out of here!”

Exclamations flew from all sides. Raskolnikov, silent, never ceased to gaze at Sonia, except to cast a rapid glance now and then at Luzhin. The girl, motionless in her place, seemed more stupefied than surprised. Suddenly she blushed and covered her face with her hands.

"No, it wasn't me! I didn't take anything! I don't know!" she cried in a heartrending voice, and she rushed to Catherine Ivanovna, who opened her arms to the unfortunate creature like an inviolable asylum.

"Sonia, Sonia, I don't believe it! You see, I don't believe it!" repeated Katerina Ivanovna, in spite of the evidence. Her words were accompanied by a thousand caresses; she kissed the girl, took her hands, and rocked her in her arms like a child. "You, have taken something! Why, these people are fools! Oh, Lord! You are fools, fools!" she cried to all the persons present, "you don't know what this heart is, what this girl is! She, steal! She! Why, she'd sell her last rag, she'd go barefoot rather than let you suffer, if you were in need; that's what she is! She took the yellow ticket because my children were starving, she sold herself for us! Ah, my poor dead husband, my poor dead husband! Lord! But defend her, defend her, all of you, instead of standing by like idiots! Rodion Romanovitch, why don't you take her part? Are you, too, like them, against her? Why, all of you are worth less than her little finger! Oh, Lord! Defend her, defend her!"

The tears, the supplications, the despair of the poor Catherine Ivanovna seemed to make a deep impression on the public. That phthisic face, those dry lips, that extinguished voice expressed such poignant suffering that it was difficult not to be touched. Pierre Petrovitch immediately returned to more tender feelings:

"Madam, madam," he said solemnly, "this affair does not concern you in any way! No one is thinking of accusing you of complicity; besides, it was you yourself who, by turning the pockets inside out, discovered the stolen object: that is enough to establish your complete innocence. I am quite willing to show indulgence for an act to which poverty may have led Sophie Semyonovna, but why, mademoiselle, did you not want to confess? Did you fear dishonor? Was it your first step? Perhaps you had lost your head? The thing is understandable, it is very understandable... See, however, what you were exposing yourself to! Gentlemen!" he said to the assistants, moved by a feeling of pity, "I am ready to forgive even now, despite the personal insults that have been addressed to me." Then he added, turning again to Sonia: "Mademoiselle, let the humiliation of today serve as a lesson for the future; I will not give any further follow-up to this affair, things will remain as they are. That's enough."

Pyotr Petrovitch glanced down at Raskolnikov. Their eyes met, and the young man's eyes flashed. As for Katerina Ivanovna, she seemed not to have heard anything, and she continued to embrace Sonia with a sort of frenzy. The children, following their mother's example, clasped the young girl in their little arms; Poletchka, not understanding what was going on, was sobbing bitterly; her pretty face, all wet with tears, was pressed against Sonia's shoulder. Suddenly a loud voice was heard on the threshold:

— How low that is!

Pierre Petrovitch turned round quickly.

"What a low trick!" repeated Lebeziatnikoff, looking fixedly at Luzhin.

The latter felt a sort of shiver. Everyone noticed it (they remembered it later). Lebeziatnikov entered the room.

“And you dared to invoke my testimony?” he said, approaching the businessman.

“What does that mean, Andrei Semenovich? What are you talking about?” mumbled Luzhin.

"That means that you are a... slanderer, that's what my words mean!" Lebeziatnikov retorted with heat. He was in a violent rage, and, while he fixed Peter Petrovich with his gaze, his little sick eyes had an unusual expression of hardness. Raskolnikov listened eagerly, his eyes fixed on the face of the young socialist.

There was a silence. At first, Pyotr Petrovich was almost disconcerted.

"If you are speaking to me," he stammered, "what is the matter with you? Are you in your right mind?"

"Yes, I am in my right mind, and you are... a scoundrel! Ah! how low! I heard everything, and if I did not speak sooner, it is because I wanted to understand everything; there are still, I admit, things that I do not explain very well to myself... I wonder why you did all this.

“You low-born fellow, if either of us has been drinking, it is you rather than I! I never take brandy, because it is contrary to my principles! Just think, it was he, he himself, who gave this hundred-rouble note to Sophia Semenovna with his own hand—I saw it, I was a witness, I will swear to it! It was he, he!” repeated Lebeziatnikoff, addressing himself to everybody and nobody.

"Are you mad, you little fool?" cried Luzhin, angrily. "She herself, just now, before you, before everybody, declared that she had only received ten roubles from me. How can it be that I gave her more?"

I saw it, I saw it! repeated Andrei Semenovich with energy.

— and although it is contrary to my principles, I am ready to swear it before the courts: I saw you slip that money to him on the sly! Only I thought, in my stupidity, that you were acting thus out of generosity! At the moment when you were saying goodbye to him on the threshold of the door, and while you were offering him your right hand, you quietly slipped a paper into his pocket which you were holding in your left hand. I saw it! I saw it!

Loujine turned pale.

“What a story you're telling us!” he replied insolently. “Being near the window, how could you have seen that paper? Your bad eyes have been the plaything of an illusion… you were dazzled, that's all!”

"No, I was not mistaken! And, in spite of the distance, I saw everything, everything! From the window, it was indeed difficult to distinguish the paper,—in that respect your observation is correct,—but, owing to a particular circumstance, I knew that it was precisely a hundred-rouble note. When you gave ten roubles to Sophie Semenovna, I was then near the table, and I saw you take a hundred-rouble note at the same time. I did not forget this detail because at that moment an idea occurred to me. After folding the note, you held it tightly in the hollow of your hand. Then I forgot it, but when you got up, you transferred the paper from your right hand to your left, and nearly dropped it. I immediately recalled the thing, because the same idea had come back to me: namely, that you wanted to oblige Sophie Semenovna without my knowledge. You can imagine with what attention I began to observe your actions—well, I saw that you put the note in her pocket. I saw it, I saw it, I will swear to it!"

Lebeziatnikoff was almost suffocated with indignation. Various exclamations were heard from all sides; most of them expressed astonishment, but some were uttered in a threatening tone. The bystanders crowded around Peter Petrovich. Catherine Ivanovna rushed to Lebeziatnikoff.

“Andrey Semyonovitch! I had misjudged you! You are defending her! You are the only one who takes her part! It is God who sent you to the aid of the orphan! Andrey Semyonovitch, my dear friend, my little father!”

And Catherine Ivanovna, hardly aware of what she was doing, fell to her knees before the young man.

"That's nonsense!" cried Luzhin, beside himself with anger. "You are talking nonsense, sir."

— "I forgot, I remembered, I remembered, I forgot"; what does that mean? So, according to you, I would have deliberately slipped a hundred roubles into his pocket? Why? For what purpose? What do I have in common with this...

"Why?" I do not understand myself, I confine myself to telling the fact as it happened, without pretending to explain it, and, within these limits, I guarantee its entire accuracy! I am so little mistaken, vile criminal, that I remember asking myself this question at the very moment when I was congratulating you and shaking your hand. I asked myself why you had made this gift in a clandestine manner. Perhaps, I said to myself, he wanted to hide his good deed from me, knowing that I am by principle an enemy of private charity, that I regard it as a vain palliative. Then I thought that you had wanted to surprise Sophie Semenovna: there are, in fact, people who like to give their benefits the flavor of the unexpected. Then another idea came to me: your intention was perhaps to test the young girl; you wanted to know if, when she had found these hundred roubles in her pocket, she would come to thank you. Or did you only intend to escape her gratitude, following the precept that the right hand must not know ... In short, God knows all the suppositions that have come to my mind! Your conduct intrigued me so much that I proposed to think about it later at leisure; in the meantime, I would have thought it lacking in delicacy if I had let you see that I knew your secret. Meanwhile, a fear came to me: Sophie Semenovna, not being informed of your generosity, could by chance lose the banknote. That's why I decided to come here, I wanted to take her aside and tell her that you had put a hundred roubles in her pocket. But before, I went to the Kobyliatnikoff ladies to give them aGeneral View of the Positive Methodand recommend to them particularly the article by Pidérit (Wagner's is not without value either). A moment later, I arrived here and was witness to this affair! Come, could I have had all these ideas, made all these reasonings, if I had not seen you slip the hundred roubles into Sophie Semenovna's pocket?

When Andrei Semenovich finished his speech, he was exhausted and his face was streaming with sweat. Alas! Even in Russian, he had difficulty expressing himself properly, although, for the rest, he knew no other language. So this oratorical effort had exhausted him. His words nevertheless produced an extraordinary effect. The accent of sincerity with which he had pronounced them carried conviction into the souls of all the listeners. Pierre Petrovitch felt that the ground was becoming bad for him.

“What do I care about the silly questions that came into your head!” he cried. “That is not proof! You may have dreamed all these absurdities! I tell you that you are lying, sir! You are lying and slandering me to satisfy a grudge! The truth is that you are angry with me because I have resisted the impious radicalism of your antisocial doctrines!”

But far from turning to the advantage of Pierre Petrovitch, this attack only provoked violent murmurs around him.

Ah! That's all you can say! That's not very clever! said Lebeziatnikoff.

— Call the police, I will swear! One thing remains obscure to me, it is the motive that pushed him to commit such a low action! Oh! the wretch, the coward!

Raskolnikov emerged from the crowd.

"I can explain his conduct, and, if necessary, I will swear to it too!" he said in a firm voice.

At first sight, the young man's calm assurance proved to the public that he knew the ins and outs of the affair, and that this imbroglio was coming to a head.

Now I understand everything," continued Raskolnikov, addressing Lebeziatnikoff directly.

"From the very beginning of this affair, I suspected some vile intrigue, and my suspicions were based on certain circumstances known to me alone, which I shall now reveal, for they show this affair in its true light. It was you, Andrei Semenovich, who, by your valuable testimony, finally threw light on my mind. I beg everyone to listen. This gentleman," he continued, pointing to Pyotr Petrovich, "has recently asked for the hand of my sister, Avdotia Romanovna Raskolnikov. This man is very wicked... "This money," I said, "I gave to Catherine Ivanovna to pay for her husband's funeral, and not to Sofya Semyonovna, whose face I did not even know until today." If he had succeeded in convincing her of theft, it was I who would have become guilty in the eyes of my mother and sister, since I had not feared to compromise the latter in the company of a thief; he, on the contrary, by attacking me, saved the consideration of my sister, his future wife. In short, it was for him a way to estrange me from my family and to get back in their graces. Raskolnikov ended his speech on these words, frequently interrupted by the exclamations of an audience, which was, however, very attentive. But, in spite of the interruptions, his speech retained to the end a calm, assurance, an unperturbed clarity. His vibrant voice, his convinced accent and his severe face deeply moved the audience.

Yes, yes, that's it! Lébéziatnikoff hastened to agree.

“You must be right, for at the very moment Sophie Semyonovna entered our room, he asked me specifically if you were here, if I had seen you among the guests of Catherine Ivanovna. He drew me into the window recess to ask me this question in a whisper. So he needed you to be there! Yes, that's it!”

Pyotr Petrovitch, very pale, remained silent and smiled contemptuously. He seemed to be thinking of some way to get out of the situation. Perhaps he would have gladly slipped away at once, but at that moment retreat was almost impossible: to leave would have been to admit implicitly the truth of the accusations against him, to confess himself guilty of slandering Sofya Semyonovna. On the other hand, the attitude of the public, excited by copious libations, was anything but reassuring. The official from the commissariat, though he had not a very clear idea of the matter, was shouting louder than anyone and proposing certain measures which were very disagreeable to Pyotr Petrovitch. Besides, there were not only drunkards in the room; many of the lodgers who had not dined at Katerina Ivanovna's had joined in the general commotion. The three Poles, greatly excited, were continually shouting and gesticulating. Pyotr Petrovitch found himself in a very unpleasant position.

Sonia listened with intense attention, but seemed not to have yet recovered her full presence of mind; one would have said that the young girl had just come out of a swoon. She did not take her eyes off Raskolnikov, feeling that all her support was in him. Catherine Ivanovna seemed very ill; every time she breathed, a hoarse sound escaped from her chest.

The most foolish figure was that of Amalia Ivanovna. The landlady had the air of not understanding anything and, with her mouth wide open, looked around in amazement. She saw only that Pyotr Petrovich was in a bad way. Raskolnikov wanted to speak again, but he had to give up, unable to make himself heard. From all sides insults and threats rained down on Luzhin, around whom a group as hostile as it was compact had formed. The businessman kept up a good front. Realizing that the game was definitely lost for him, he resorted to effrontery.

"Pardon, gentlemen, pardon, don't push like that, let me through," he said, trying to make his way through the crowd.

"It is useless, I assure you, to try to intimidate me with your threats, I am not afraid for so little. The judges are enlightened people and ... not drunk: they will reject the testimony of two impious men, two proven revolutionaries who accuse me for the purpose of personal revenge, as they themselves have the stupidity to recognize ... — I don't want to breathe the same air as you, and I ask you to leave my room, everything is over between us! When I think that for fifteen days I have sweated blood and water to expose him…

He succeeded in making his way through the crowd; but the purveyor, finding that abuse was not sufficient punishment, took a glass from the table and flung it with all his force at Pyotr Petrovitch. Unfortunately, the missile intended for the business man struck Amalia Ivanovna, who uttered a piercing scream. In brandishing the glass, the purveyor lost his balance and rolled heavily under the table. Luzhin went back to Lebeziatnikoff's, and an hour later left the house.

Naturally timid, Sonia, even before this adventure, knew that her situation exposed her to all attacks and that anyone could insult her almost with impunity. However, until then she had hoped to disarm the malice by force of circumspection, gentleness, humility before everyone and everyone. Now this illusion was escaping her. No doubt, she had enough patience to bear even this with resignation and almost without murmuring, but at the moment the disappointment was too cruel. Although her innocence had triumphed over the slander, when her first fear passed, when she was able to realize things, her heart was painfully squeezed at the thought of her abandonment, of her isolation in life. The young girl had a nervous breakdown. In the end, no longer possessing herself, she fled the room and returned home in haste. Her departure took place shortly after that of Luzhin.

The accident that had befallen Amalia Ivanovna had caused general hilarity, but the landlady took it very badly and turned her anger against Catherine Ivanovna, who, overcome by suffering, had had to lie down on her bed:

— Get out of here! Right away! Forward, march!

While she was speaking these words in an irritated voice, Madame Lippevechzel seized all the objects belonging to her lodger and threw them in a heap on the floor. Broken, almost fainting, the poor Catherine Ivanovna jumped out of bed and threw herself on Amalia Ivanovna. But the struggle was too unequal; the landlady had no trouble in repulsing this attack.

“How is it that she has not contented herself with slandering Sonia, but must also attack me? What! on the very day of my husband's funeral, after having received my hospitality, she throws me out into the street with my children! But where shall I go?” cried the poor woman, sobbing. “Lord!” she exclaimed suddenly, with flashing eyes, “is there then no justice in the world? Whom wilt thou defend, if thou dost not defend us, who are orphans? But we shall see! There are judges and courts of justice on earth, and I will appeal to them! Wait a little, you wicked creature! Poletchka, stay with the children, I will come back. If they turn you out, wait for me in the street! We shall see whether there is justice in this world!”

Catherine Ivanovna put on her head the same green handkerchief of "lady's cloth" which had been mentioned in Marmeladov's story; then she pushed her way through the drunken and noisy crowd of lodgers who were still crowding the room, and, with her face bathed in tears, went out into the street with the firm intention of going somewhere, at all costs, to seek justice. Poletchka, frightened, pressed her little brother and sister to her; the three children, huddled together in the corner near the chest, waited trembling for their mother's return.

Amalia Ivanovna, like a fury, was pacing the room, screaming with rage and throwing everything she could lay her hands on to the floor. Some of the lodgers were discussing the event; others were quarreling; still others were singing songs.

"It is time I went," thought Raskolnikov.

— "Well, Sophie Semyonovna, we'll see what you have to say now!"

And he went to Sonia's house.

They were sitting side by side, sad and dejected, like two shipwrecked sailors thrown by the storm on a deserted shore. Looking at Sonia, Raskolnikov felt how much she loved him, and, strangely, this immense tenderness of which he saw himself the object suddenly caused him a painful impression. He had gone to Sonia, saying to himself that his only refuge, his only hope was in her; he had yielded to an irresistible need to pour out his sorrow; now that the young girl had given him her whole heart, he admitted to himself that he was infinitely more unhappy than before.

"Sonia," he said, "it is better for you not to come to see me during my detention."

Sonia did not answer; she was weeping. A few minutes passed.

"Have you a cross on you?" she asked suddenly, as if struck by a sudden idea.

At first he did not understand the question.

“No, you don't have one? Well, take this one, it's made of cypress wood. I have another one made of copper, which came from Elisabeth. We made an exchange, she gave me her cross and I gave her an image. I will now wear Elisabeth's cross, and you will wear this one. Take it… it's mine!” she insisted. “We will go together to atonement, together we will carry the cross.”

“Give it to me,” said Raskolnikov, so as not to hurt her feelings, and he held out his hand, but almost immediately drew it back.

"Not now, Sonia. Later, it will be better," he added by way of concession.

“Yes, yes, later,” she replied with warmth, “I will give it to you at the moment of expiation. You will come to my house, I will put it around your neck, we will say a prayer, and then we will leave.”

At the same moment, three knocks were struck on the door.

"Sophie Semenovna, may I come in?" said a pleasant and familiar voice.

Sonia, worried, ran to open the door. The visitor was none other than Lebeziatnikov.
